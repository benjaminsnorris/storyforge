# Structural Craft — The Architecture of Story

This document covers the structural principles that the elaboration pipeline tracks and validates. These are the load-bearing elements of narrative — the decisions that determine whether a story works before a word of prose is written.

Where the craft engine (craft-engine.md) covers how to write well, this document covers how to build well. A beautifully written scene that doesn't turn a value, advance a thread, or change a character's knowledge state is a decorated hallway that leads nowhere.

---

## 1. Value Shift (Robert McKee)

### The Principle

Every scene must change the value-charged condition of a character's life. A "value" is an abstract quality that can be positively or negatively charged: safety, love, justice, truth, freedom, honor, loyalty. The shift is the movement from one charge to another.

If a scene begins with the character safe and ends with the character safe, nothing happened. The reader's time was wasted. McKee calls this a "nonevent" — the most damning structural diagnosis a scene can receive.

### The Notation

- **+/−** — positive to negative. The character had something and lost it.
- **−/+** — negative to positive. The character lacked something and gained it.
- **+/++** — positive to more positive. Things were good and got better.
- **−/−−** — negative to worse. Things were bad and got worse.
- **+/+** or **−/−** — flat. Nothing changed. This is the problem case.

### What Good Looks Like

A well-structured manuscript shows a clear trajectory when you read the value shifts in sequence. Most stories follow a pattern: the protagonist starts in a reasonably stable position (+), is destabilized (−), reaches a low point (−−), then either recovers (+) or doesn't (tragedy remains at − or −−). The midpoint often marks the shift from reactive to proactive — the first scene where the value shifts upward after a sustained decline.

The best scenes shift on multiple values simultaneously. A scene where the character gains safety but loses trust is richer than one where only safety changes.

### What Bad Looks Like

- **Flat stretches:** Three or more consecutive scenes with the same polarity (+/+ or −/−). The story has stalled. Something needs to go wrong (or right) to restart movement.
- **Monotonic descent:** Every scene is −/+ or −/−−. Relentless negativity without relief creates reader exhaustion, not tension. Even dark stories need moments of +/− (gaining something before losing it again) to create the rhythm of hope and loss.
- **Unearned reversals:** A −− to ++ in a single scene. Major shifts need to be earned across multiple scenes. A character who is devastated in one scene and triumphant in the next hasn't been through enough to justify the reversal.

### How to Fix It

If a scene has a flat value shift, ask: what could go wrong here? What does the character stand to lose? If they're already in a bad position, what small gain could be threatened? The shift doesn't have to be dramatic — a character who enters a scene trusting someone and exits with a seed of doubt has shifted from + to +/−, and that's enough.

### The Value at Stake

Naming the value at stake forces precision. "Things get worse" is vague. "Her sense of safety erodes because the man she relied on for protection is the one who arranged the threat" is specific and testable. The value (safety) is identified, the shift direction (−) is clear, and the mechanism (betrayal by a protector) is concrete.

Varying values across sequences prevents fatigue. If five consecutive scenes all test "justice," the reader starts to feel lectured. Alternating between justice, safety, trust, and loyalty creates a richer emotional texture even when the plot is focused on a single conflict.

## 2. Scene Structure (Dwight Swain)

### The Principle

Swain identified two fundamental scene types that alternate to create narrative rhythm:

**Action scenes** have three components:
1. **Goal** — the character wants something concrete and specific
2. **Conflict** — something opposes the goal
3. **Outcome** — the scene resolves in one of four ways: yes, no, yes-but, no-and

**Sequel scenes** have three components:
1. **Reaction** — the character processes what just happened emotionally
2. **Dilemma** — the character weighs options, each with costs
3. **Decision** — the character chooses a course of action, which becomes the goal of the next action scene

### The Rhythm

Action and sequel alternate like breathing — action is the exhale (tension, pursuit, opposition), sequel is the inhale (processing, weighing, deciding). The ratio between them controls pacing:

- **Thriller pacing:** Short or absent sequels. Action → action → brief sequel → action. The reader barely has time to process before the next crisis.
- **Literary pacing:** Extended sequels with rich interiority. Action → long sequel → action. The reader lives inside the character's processing.
- **Balanced pacing:** Roughly equal weight. Each action scene gets a sequel that earns the next action.

### The Four Outcomes

The outcome determines forward momentum:

- **Yes** — the character gets what they wanted. Tension drops. Use sparingly — too many "yes" outcomes and there's no conflict.
- **No** — the character fails. Creates frustration that needs a sequel to process.
- **Yes, but** — the character succeeds, but a new complication arises. The most useful outcome for maintaining momentum. They got the information they needed, but now someone knows they have it.
- **No, and furthermore** — the character fails, and things get worse. The most useful outcome for escalation. Not only did the judge dismiss the evidence, but now the judge knows they're investigating.

Most scenes in a well-paced manuscript end in "yes-but" or "no-and." These are the outcomes that create the forward pressure readers describe as "I couldn't put it down."

### What Bad Looks Like

- **Four or more consecutive action scenes:** The reader is breathless but emotionally shallow. Nothing is being processed. The characters feel like action figures, not people.
- **Four or more consecutive sequels:** The reader is drowning in interiority. Nothing is happening. The characters feel like they're journaling instead of living.
- **All "yes" outcomes:** No tension. The character succeeds at everything. Why is the reader worried?
- **All "no" outcomes:** No progress. The character fails at everything. Why is the reader hopeful?

### How to Fix It

If you have a run of action scenes, find the scene where the emotional impact is strongest and insert a sequel after it. The character needs to process what happened before charging into the next confrontation.

If you have a run of sequels, look for the decision that ends each one and ask: can this decision lead directly into an action scene? The sequel's decision should create a new goal that the next scene pursues.

## 3. Turning Points (Story Grid)

### The Principle

Every scene turns — changes from one state to another — through a turning point. There are exactly two types:

**Action turning points:** A character *does something* that changes the situation. They make a phone call. They open a door. They pull a trigger. The change comes from within the scene through character agency.

**Revelation turning points:** New *information* changes the situation. A character learns something. A secret is exposed. A letter arrives. The change comes from outside through discovery.

### Why Variety Matters

If every scene turns on revelation (the character learns something new), the manuscript reads as a series of information deliveries. The characters are passive recipients of plot. Scenes feel like briefings.

If every scene turns on action (a character does something), the manuscript reads as a series of events without discovery. There's no surprise, no recontextualization of what came before.

The best manuscripts alternate. A revelation scene (she discovers the survey was falsified) sets up an action scene (she rides to confront the judge). The revelation creates the conditions; the action changes them.

### What Bad Looks Like

- **Four or more consecutive revelations:** "And then she found out... and then she discovered... and then he told her..." The characters are learning machines, not agents.
- **Four or more consecutive actions:** "And then he went... and then she did... and then they fought..." The characters are doing things without moments of discovery or realization that give the action meaning.

### How to Fix It

If you have a run of revelations, look for a scene where the character acts on what they've learned instead of learning more. They have enough information — now they need to do something with it.

If you have a run of actions, look for a scene where something the character discovers changes the meaning of what they've been doing. They've been fighting the wrong enemy. The evidence points somewhere unexpected. A quiet realization shifts everything.

## 4. The Five Commandments (Story Grid / Shawn Coyne)

### The Principle

Every working scene contains five elements:

1. **Inciting Incident** — something happens that upsets the balance. Either *causal* (a character causes it) or *coincidental* (it just happens). The scene's clock starts here.

2. **Progressive Complications** — obstacles escalate. Each complication is worse than the last. The pressure builds toward a moment that demands a response. The final progressive complication is the scene's turning point (action or revelation).

3. **Crisis** — the character faces a dilemma. There are exactly two types:
   - **Best bad choice** — two options, both with costs. Whichever they choose, they lose something.
   - **Irreconcilable goods** — two desirable outcomes that cannot coexist. Choosing one means giving up the other.

4. **Climax** — the character chooses. This is not the most exciting moment — it's the moment of *decision*. The character acts under pressure. What they choose reveals who they are.

5. **Resolution** — consequences of the choice play out. The new conditions are established. These conditions become the starting state of the next scene.

### Why the Crisis Matters Most

The crisis is where the scene earns its dramatic weight. A scene without a genuine dilemma is a scene where the "right answer" is obvious — the character just does the obviously correct thing and moves on. This is satisfying for the character but boring for the reader.

The best crises are agonizing. Both options are defensible. Reasonable people would disagree about which choice is right. The character's specific wound, lie, and values determine which way they go — and that choice illuminates who they are more powerfully than any amount of description or interiority.

### What Bad Looks Like

- **No crisis:** The character faces a "dilemma" where one option is clearly right and the other is clearly wrong. There's no tension in the choice. "Should I save the child or eat lunch?" is not a crisis.
- **Skipped climax:** The scene jumps from crisis to resolution without showing the character's active choice. Things just... work out. Or don't. The character didn't drive the outcome.
- **No inciting incident:** The scene starts in the middle of complications with no setup. The reader doesn't know what's at stake because the balance was never established.

### How to Fix It

If the crisis feels weak, make both options cost something real. What does the character lose if they choose A? What do they lose if they choose B? If one option has no cost, it's not a real dilemma.

If the climax feels passive, restructure so the character must act. Even choosing not to act can be active — but only if the character consciously decides to hold back, not if they simply fail to do anything.

## 5. MICE Threads (Mary Robinette Kowal)

### The Principle

Every story thread is one of four types:

- **Milieu** — opens when a character enters a space, closes when they leave. A journey into a dangerous territory. An investigation that takes the character into an unfamiliar world. The thread resolves when they exit.
- **Inquiry** — opens with a question, closes with an answer. "Who killed the surveyor?" opens the thread. "Judge Keele ordered it" closes it. The thread resolves when the question is answered (whether or not the answer is satisfying).
- **Character** — opens with dissatisfaction, closes with acceptance or change. A character who is unhappy with their role in life. The thread resolves when they either change or accept their situation.
- **Event** — opens with a disruption of the status quo, closes when a new normal is established. A murder disrupts a town. The thread resolves when order is restored (or a new order replaces the old).

### The Nesting Rule (FILO)

MICE threads must close in **reverse order** of opening — First In, Last Out, like nested parentheses:

```
+milieu:dangerous-territory
  +inquiry:who-killed-the-surveyor
    +character:protagonist-transformation
    -character:protagonist-transformation
  -inquiry:who-killed-the-surveyor
-milieu:dangerous-territory
```

If you open milieu first, then inquiry, you must close inquiry before closing milieu. Opening A, opening B, then closing A before B feels wrong to readers even if they can't articulate why — it's like closing an outer parenthesis before an inner one.

### Why It Matters

Thread management is what separates novels that feel "complete" from novels that feel like they're missing something. An open thread is a promise to the reader. Every `+` says "I'm going to resolve this." If you don't close it, or if you close things in the wrong order, the reader feels cheated — even subconsciously.

Thread count also affects complexity. Six to eleven active threads is the sweet spot for a novel. Fewer than six feels thin. More than eleven overwhelms the reader's ability to track them all.

### What Bad Looks Like

- **Orphaned threads:** A thread opens and never closes. "Who sent the mysterious letter?" is asked in Chapter 3 and never answered.
- **Premature closes:** A thread closes before it's been developed enough to earn the resolution. The mystery is solved in two scenes — the question didn't have time to build tension.
- **FILO violations:** Threads close in the wrong order. The outer journey resolves before the inner question is answered, creating a structural dissonance.
- **Thread dormancy:** A thread goes more than 8-10 scenes without being referenced. The reader forgets it exists. When it reappears, it feels like a discontinuity.

### How to Fix It

If a thread is dormant, add a brief reference — a character's passing thought, a detail in the environment, a line of dialogue that touches the thread without resolving it. This keeps it alive in the reader's awareness.

If FILO is violated, consider whether you can reorder the closings. Sometimes a scene that closes thread A can be moved after the scene that closes thread B. If reordering isn't possible, the nesting structure may need redesign — which is exactly the kind of problem that's cheap to fix in the CSV and expensive to fix in prose.

## 6. Knowledge Flow

### The Principle

Characters exist in a state of partial knowledge. They know what they've experienced and what they've been told — nothing more. When a character acts on information they haven't received, the reader's trust breaks. When a character fails to act on information they have, the reader's trust also breaks (unless the failure is characterologically motivated).

Knowledge flow tracks, per scene, what each POV character knows entering and leaving. The difference between `knowledge_in` and `knowledge_out` is what the character learns during the scene. This creates a chain: scene N's `knowledge_out` becomes available for scene N+1's `knowledge_in`.

### Why It Matters

Knowledge flow is the mechanical backbone of continuity. It enables:

- **Dramatic irony:** The reader knows something a character doesn't (because the reader experienced an earlier scene from a different POV). This is only possible if you track what each character knows.
- **Information management:** In mysteries and thrillers, controlling when characters learn what is the entire plot structure. Knowledge flow makes this trackable rather than intuitive.
- **Parallel drafting:** Scenes with clearly defined knowledge_in can be drafted independently because their starting conditions are fully specified. This is what enables wave-based parallel drafting.

### What Bad Looks Like

- **Knowledge violations:** A character references information from a scene they weren't in and haven't been told about. "But how did she know about the letter? She wasn't there when he opened it."
- **Knowledge paralysis:** A character has information that should motivate action but doesn't use it. Unless there's a clear reason for the delay (fear, denial, strategy), this reads as a plot hole.
- **Wording drift:** The knowledge chain uses different words for the same fact across scenes, making it impossible to validate automatically. "The survey was falsified" in one scene and "Keele suppressed the survey data" in another — same fact, different words, broken validation.

### How to Fix It

Use exact wording across the knowledge chain. If scene 5's `knowledge_out` says "the eastern readings don't match," scene 12's `knowledge_in` should use that exact phrase, not a paraphrase. This is a concession to machine validation that also improves human readability — you can grep for a specific fact and find every scene where it's in play.

If validation flags a knowledge violation, determine: is the character actually in a position to know this? If yes, trace back to find where they learned it and ensure the chain is connected. If no, either add a scene where they learn it or remove the reference.

## 7. Continuity Dependencies

### The Principle

Not all scenes depend on each other. Many scenes are independent — they share the same story world but don't directly pass information between them. Identifying which scenes actually depend on which others enables parallel drafting and makes the story's information architecture visible.

A scene depends on another if it references facts established in that scene's `knowledge_out`. The `continuity_deps` column lists these dependencies explicitly.

### Why It Matters

- **Parallel drafting:** Scenes with no dependencies (wave 1) can all be drafted simultaneously. Scenes that depend only on wave 1 scenes form wave 2, and so on. A 60-scene novel might only need 3-5 waves, reducing drafting time dramatically.
- **Structural visibility:** The dependency graph reveals the information architecture of the story. Scenes that many other scenes depend on are load-bearing — if they change, cascading re-drafts are needed. Scenes with no dependents are structurally isolated — they can change freely.
- **Revision targeting:** When evaluation finds a problem in a scene, the dependency graph shows exactly which downstream scenes need to be checked for cascade effects.

### How to Minimize Dependencies

Fewer dependencies means more parallelism. Ask: does this scene really need to reference that specific prior scene, or could the relevant fact be established through `knowledge_in` without a direct dependency? Sometimes a character's starting knowledge can include a fact "from offscreen" — something they learned between scenes — which removes a dependency without creating a continuity error.

## 8. Structural Scoring

### Pre-Draft Scoring

Before any prose exists, the elaboration data can be scored:

- Does every scene have a non-flat value shift?
- Is the goal/conflict/outcome chain complete?
- Is the crisis a genuine dilemma?
- Do turning point types vary across sequences?
- Is the action/sequel rhythm balanced?
- Do knowledge states connect properly?

A scene scoring 5/5 on structural scoring has all the bones it needs. A scene scoring 0/5 has no brief data at all. The score doesn't predict prose quality — it predicts whether the scene has the structural foundation to support good prose.

### Post-Draft Fidelity Scoring

After drafting, fidelity scoring checks whether the prose delivered what the brief promised. Nine elements are scored: goal, conflict, outcome, crisis, decision, key_actions, key_dialogue, emotions, knowledge.

A fidelity score of 4 means the prose clearly delivers the brief element. A score of 5 means the prose found something better than what the brief specified — the writer discovered a superior approach during drafting. A score of 1-2 means the brief element is missing or barely present in the prose.

Low fidelity scores point to one of two problems: either the brief was right and the prose needs revision, or the prose is right and the brief needs updating. The author decides which.

## Putting It Together

These principles work together. A scene that turns a value (McKee) does so through an action scene's outcome or a sequel scene's decision (Swain). The turn happens at a specific point — either an action or a revelation (Story Grid). The scene contains all five commandments, with the crisis as the dramatic center. The threads it opens and closes are tracked in proper nesting order (Kowal). The character's knowledge entering and leaving is explicit and validated.

When all of these elements are working, the author can focus on the prose — voice, rhythm, imagery, dialogue — knowing that the structural foundation is sound. The elaboration pipeline exists to make this structural work visible, trackable, and fixable before expensive prose decisions are made.
