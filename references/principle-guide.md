# Principle Guide — What It Looks Like, What It Doesn't

This document provides concise, evaluator-facing descriptions of each scene-level craft principle. Each entry describes what the principle looks like when executed well, what it looks like when it fails, and what specific textual evidence to look for. These descriptions are designed for inclusion in scoring prompts.

---

## Scene Craft

### enter_late_leave_early

**When it works:** The scene drops the reader into a moment already charged with tension, conflict, or consequence. No arrival, no settling in, no pleasantries. The reader is in the room when the argument starts. The scene ends on or just after the turn — the moment of change, revelation, or decision — trusting the reader to carry the emotional weight forward. What is omitted between scenes creates meaning.

**When it fails:** The scene opens with setup: a character arriving, sitting down, ordering coffee, exchanging greetings, thinking about what's about to happen. The reader wades through a paragraph or more before anything is at stake. After the turn, the scene lingers — the character reflects on what just happened, restates the scene's meaning, or narrates the transition to the next location. The exits and entrances are visible as mechanics, not as craft.

**What to look for:** Compare the first moment of tension to the first line of the scene — how many words separate them? After the scene's turning point, does the scene continue? Could the opening or closing be cut without the reader losing anything essential?

---

### every_scene_must_turn

**When it works:** Something is different at the end of the scene than at the beginning. A relationship shifts. Information reframes understanding. A plan fails or a commitment deepens. The scene has a question ("Will she confront him?") and an answer ("She tries but loses her nerve — and he notices"). The change is earned by the scene's own logic, not by coincidence or external interruption. Removing this scene would leave a gap the reader would feel.

**When it fails:** The scene is a static tableau. Characters talk, observe, or think, but nothing changes. No relationship shifts, no information is revealed, no commitment breaks or deepens. If asked "what changed?", the answer is nothing or something trivially minor. The scene could be removed without affecting the story. Alternatively, the turn is muddled — something technically changes but doesn't register emotionally.

**What to look for:** Can you state the scene's question and answer? What specific value (relationship, knowledge, status, power, trust) is different at the end? Is the change produced by the scene's internal events or imposed from outside?

---

### scene_emotion_vs_character

**When it works:** There is a gap between what the POV character feels and what the scene makes the reader feel. A calm character in mounting danger creates dramatic irony. An elated character heading toward betrayal creates anticipatory grief. The gap is built through detail selection, juxtaposition, and pacing — not through authorial announcement. The reader's emotional experience is richer and more layered than the character's internal state.

**When it fails:** Scene emotion and character emotion are identical. When the character is happy, the scene feels happy. There is no irony, no dramatic tension between knowledge levels, no undercurrents. Or, the gap is created through heavy-handed foreshadowing ("she didn't know it would be the last time...") rather than through the scene's own construction. The scene is emotionally one-dimensional — the reader feels only what they are told to feel.

**What to look for:** What does the character feel? What does the reader feel? Are they different? If there is a gap, how is it created — through craft (detail, juxtaposition, what's left unsaid) or through authorial intrusion (narrator telling the reader what to feel)?

---

### psychic_distance_scene

**When it works:** The scene opens with enough distance to orient the reader — where, when, what physical reality. Then narrows as the scene intensifies. The climactic moment is at the closest distance the style permits: inside the character's body, feeling their sensations, seeing through their eyes. The pull-back after the turn is measured, not abrupt. The distance arc mirrors the emotional arc.

**When it fails:** The scene sits at one flat distance throughout — usually middle. The setup and the climax are narrated from the same remove. Or distance lurches randomly: a sudden dive into stream-of-consciousness for one paragraph, then back to detached observation. The climactic moment is narrated at arm's length when the reader needs to be inside it.

**What to look for:** What is the psychic distance of the opening lines? The closing lines? The scene's most intense moment? Does the distance narrow gradually or shift abruptly? Is the closest distance reserved for the moment of highest emotional stakes?

---

### show_vs_tell_scenes

**When it works:** The scene's pivotal emotional moment is dramatized — the reader experiences it in real time through action, dialogue, sensory detail. Setup and aftermath are told efficiently. Summary serves pacing ("Three hours passed"). The balance is calibrated: showing for moments the reader needs to feel, telling for everything else.

**When it fails:** The turning point is summarized: "They argued terribly. She was furious." The reader is told what to feel instead of given the experience that produces feeling. Or the inverse: mundane transitions are dramatized at length while the emotional core is compressed. Telling replaces drama at precisely the moments drama matters most.

**What to look for:** Is the scene's single most important emotional moment shown (in real time, with sensory detail) or told (summarized, labeled)? When telling appears, does it serve pacing or replace needed drama? Does the prose label emotions the reader could already infer?

---

### thread_management

**When it works:** The scene advances at least one story thread and touches one or two others through subtext, environmental detail, or brief character thought. Dormant threads resurface with sensory anchors connecting to their last appearance — a detail the reader remembers, a gesture that echoes. The scene feels connected to the larger narrative web. No thread is introduced and dropped; no thread overwhelms all others.

**When it fails:** The scene is disconnected — it works in isolation but doesn't contribute to the novel's larger patterns. Threads are introduced and abandoned. A subplot from chapters ago returns with no anchor. Or, too many threads compete (more than three) and the scene loses its emotional center. Thread management feels mechanical — the writer checks boxes rather than weaving organically.

**What to look for:** Which threads does this scene advance? How many are active simultaneously? When a dormant thread resurfaces, is there a sensory or emotional link to its last appearance? Would removing this scene disconnect any thread from the narrative?

---

### pacing_variety

**When it works:** This scene's type, length, and emotional intensity create deliberate contrast or rhythm with the scenes around it. A short, sharp scene after a long one creates acceleration. A quiet scene after an intense one lets the reader breathe and deepen understanding. The scene's length serves an expressive purpose — it is as long as it needs to be and no longer.

**When it fails:** This scene is the same type, length, and intensity as the scenes on either side of it. Multiple high-tension scenes in a row produce adrenaline fatigue. Multiple quiet scenes stall momentum. Scene length is uniform — a default rather than a choice. The writer has one gear and stays in it.

**What to look for:** What type of scene is this (action, dialogue, introspective, transitional)? What type are the surrounding scenes? Does this scene's position create contrast, acceleration, deceleration, or breathing room? Is the scene's length a deliberate choice or a default?

## Prose Craft

### economy_clarity

**When it works:** Every word earns its place. The prose is specific where it matters (concrete nouns, precise verbs) and compressed where possible. No filler phrases, no throat-clearing, no hedging. Cutting any sentence would lose meaning. The prose communicates efficiently without feeling stripped.

**When it fails:** The prose carries dead weight: "the fact that," "began to," "in order to," "somewhat," "it was clear that." Sentences contain qualifications that add nothing. Descriptions are vague and general ("a large room with many windows") where they should be specific and concrete. Emotional states are labeled with adjectives rather than rendered through action. You could cut 20% of the words and lose nothing.

**What to look for:** Filler phrases and hedging language. Passages that could be tightened. Vague language where precision would serve better. Emotional labels where action would convince. The ratio of words spent to meaning delivered.

---

### sentence_as_thought

**When it works:** Each sentence carries one clear thought, and length varies to create rhythm. Short sentences punch. Long sentences flow. Paragraphs have internal shape — they build, crest, resolve. The prose reads smoothly aloud. Fragments are used for emphasis, complex sentences for nuance.

**When it fails:** Sentences pile multiple unrelated thoughts into one construction. Or all sentences are the same length and structure, producing monotonous rhythm. Paragraphs are undifferentiated blocks. The prose doesn't stumble, but it doesn't sing either. The writer has one sentence shape and uses it for everything.

**What to look for:** Run-on sentences doing the work of several. Monotonous sentence length. Paragraph shape — do they build toward something? Read a passage aloud — does the rhythm serve the content or fight it?

---

### writers_toolbox

**When it works:** Vocabulary is rich without being showy — the right word appears because the writer knows enough words. Verbs are precise and carry weight without adverb support. Metaphors are fresh, purposeful, and organic to the material. The writer can shift registers (plain to lyrical, sparse to dense) as the scene demands.

**When it fails:** Vocabulary is limited and repetitive. The same words recur. Verbs are weak, propped up by adverbs. Metaphors are cliched ("like a painting," "a canvas of red and gold") or mixed. The writer has one register and stays in it regardless of what the scene needs. Grammar is treated as an obstacle rather than an expressive tool.

**What to look for:** Repeated words or phrases. Weak verbs needing adverb support. The quality of metaphors — fresh or first-thing-that-came-to-mind? Register flexibility — does the prose shift tone when the scene's emotion shifts?

---

### precision_language

**When it works:** Punctuation controls how the reader hears the prose. Dashes create emphasis. Semicolons connect related thoughts. Colons announce. The full toolkit is deployed with purpose. When rules are broken, the breaking feels deliberate and creates effects rule-following could not.

**When it fails:** Punctuation is inconsistent, incorrect, or unadventurous. Only periods and commas appear. Comma splices, misused semicolons, or random dashes obscure meaning. The reader must re-read sentences to parse them. Or the writer is technically correct but never uses punctuation as an expressive instrument.

**What to look for:** Incorrect or inconsistent punctuation that obscures meaning. Deliberate vs. accidental rule-breaking. Range of punctuation marks used. Whether punctuation shapes the reader's experience or merely exists as mechanical correctness.

---

### persuasive_structure

**When it works:** Every paragraph earns its placement. Exposition is woven into character perception, dialogue, or action. Backstory arrives when the reader wants it — triggered by present-scene events. The reader never asks "why am I being told this?" because the answer is self-evident. Information feels like revelation, not instruction.

**When it fails:** Exposition appears in blocks disconnected from character experience — the info dump. Backstory is delivered when the writer wants to, not when the scene creates demand for it. The reader wonders why they're being told something at this point. Paragraphs feel like detours from the scene's purpose.

**What to look for:** Passages where the reader might ask "why now?" Exposition delivered as blocks vs. woven into scene. Backstory triggered by present events vs. dropped by the writer. Paragraphs that don't advance the scene's purpose.

---

### fictive_dream

**When it works:** The reader forgets they are reading. The prose is transparent — it doesn't call attention to itself. The world feels real and internally consistent. Every observation is filtered through the POV character's consciousness. Psychic distance shifts are deliberate and controlled.

**When it fails:** The dream breaks. Authorial intrusions ("As readers, we might wonder..."). Observations the POV character couldn't make. Anachronisms. Clumsy phrasing that makes the reader think about the writing rather than the story. A medieval character who thinks in modern idioms. Inconsistencies in the established world.

**What to look for:** Moments where the reader would be pulled out of the story. POV-inappropriate observations. Anachronisms or internal inconsistencies. Metaphors that feel like the author's rather than the character's. The prose calling attention to itself as prose.

---

### scene_vs_summary

**When it works:** Pivotal moments are dramatized in full — dialogue, action, sensory detail. Transitions are compressed efficiently. The reader experiences emotional turning points firsthand and accepts summary for everything else. Telling, when it appears, has authority and specificity. The balance feels natural.

**When it fails:** Critical emotional moments are compressed while trivial passages are dramatized. "The next three months were terrible" followed by two pages of grocery shopping. When telling appears in place of needed drama, it is flat and uninformative. The proportion of scene to summary is inverted — the reader is locked out of what matters and trapped inside what doesn't.

**What to look for:** The scene's most important emotional moment — is it dramatized or summarized? Told passages — do they have authority and purpose, or are they lazy compression of material that deserved scene treatment? Overall balance.

---

### sound_rhythm_pov

**When it works:** The prose reads well aloud — no stumble-points, no unintentional cacophony. Rhythm varies to serve the emotional register. POV is consistent — the world is filtered through the character's particular way of perceiving. A surgeon and a poet describe the same room differently. The character's voice and the prose's music are integrated.

**When it fails:** The prose stumbles when read aloud. Rhythm is monotonous. POV slips — the narrator reveals another character's thoughts, or makes observations the POV character couldn't make. The world is perceived generically rather than through the character's specific lens. All characters notice and describe the same way.

**What to look for:** Read passages aloud for rhythm. Check for POV violations — any access to knowledge or perceptions beyond the POV character. Assess whether the world is filtered through this specific character's consciousness or through a generic narrator's eye.

---

### permission_honesty

**When it works:** The prose commits to emotional truth. Difficult scenes are rendered with specificity and vulnerability — grief as the dead person's shoes still by the door, not as "a profound sense of loss." The writer doesn't flinch from pain, shame, desire, or joy. No retreat into abstraction, cliche, or ironic distance. Sentimentality is absent — the prose earns its emotions through honest observation.

**When it fails:** The prose avoids emotional risk. Scenes that demand vulnerability default to cliche, abstraction, or ironic distance. "She felt a profound sense of loss that words could not express" — the sentence announces emotion without producing it. The writer flinches from the hardest moment, deflecting with a joke, a subject change, or a retreat to summary. Sentimentality replaces honesty — performing feeling rather than rendering it.

**What to look for:** The scene's most emotionally difficult moment — does the prose stay with it or retreat? Specific, concrete detail vs. generic sentiment. Abstraction where physicality would convince. Ironic distance where sincerity is needed. Deflection at the moment of greatest vulnerability.

---

### prose_naturalness

**When it works:** The prose feels like a human voice thinking on the page. Sentence rhythms follow thought patterns, not rhetorical templates. Word choices feel inevitable rather than composed. The writer's technique is invisible — the reader hears a character, not an author performing craft. Contractions appear in interiority and dialogue. Sentence and paragraph lengths vary organically. The prose sounds like someone, not something.

**When it fails:** The prose feels "written" — technically proficient but recognizably artificial. Em dashes appear in every other sentence. Observations are framed as antithesis: "Not X. It's Y." or "This isn't about X — it's about Y." Parallel structures and tricolon (lists of three) recur compulsively. Paragraphs are suspiciously symmetrical in length. Hedging qualifiers stack up ("perhaps," "somewhat," "almost as if"). Scenes open with sweeping contextual statements or close with thematic summaries. The prose reads as composed rather than voiced.

**What to look for:** Em dash frequency — more than one per scene is a warning sign. Antithesis framing — contrasting pairs where the first element is negated and the second affirmed. Compulsive parallelism and tricolon. Symmetrical paragraph structure. Hedging stacks. Signposted transitions. Sweeping openers and summary closers. Absence of contractions in interiority and dialogue. Vocabulary that signals summarizing rather than rendering (multifaceted, nuanced, tapestry, palpable, pivotal, intricate, profound).

## Character Craft (Scene-Level)

### egri_premise

**When it works:** The POV character's defining trait drives the scene's central conflict. The character's nature collides with what the situation demands, and the collision creates the scene's tension. A different character in the same situation would produce a different conflict. The Egri premise is articulable: "[trait] leads to [conflict] leads to [outcome]."

**When it fails:** The plot happens to the character rather than emerging from who they are. The conflict arises from external coincidence, not from the collision between character and world. The protagonist could be replaced with a different character and the same events would unfold. The character reacts to situations rather than creating them through who they are.

**What to look for:** What is the character's defining trait in this scene? Does that trait create or shape the conflict? Would a different character produce a different outcome? Can you state the Egri premise?

---

### testing_characters

**When it works:** The POV character behaves as this specific person would — with particular reactions, avoidances, and choices that no other character would make. They have visible inner life: idiosyncrasies, contradictions, private thoughts that go beyond their plot function. Other characters respond to them specifically. The character reveals something about themselves through action, silence, or choice.

**When it fails:** The character behaves generically — as "a person" would, not as this particular person would. They react to crises with standard emotions rather than particular ones. Between plot demands, they have no visible inner life. They are defined by their role (the hero, the mentor, the love interest) rather than by their specific psychology. Other characters interact with their function, not their personality.

**What to look for:** Is the character's response to this scene's events particular or generic? Could another character respond identically? What private details emerge that go beyond plot requirements? Do other characters respond to this person specifically or to their role?

## Rules to Break (Scene-Level)

*These principles use inverted scoring: high scores mean the rule is either followed masterfully OR broken intentionally with clear artistic purpose. Low scores mean the rule is violated accidentally in ways that undermine the prose.*

### show_dont_tell

**When it works:** Emotional turning points are rendered through action, dialogue, and sensory detail — the reader experiences them. When telling appears, it serves a clear purpose: compression, transition, or a narrative voice whose authority IS the point (Austen's irony, Dickens's scope). The writer knows when the reader needs to feel and when the reader needs to be told.

**When it fails:** Emotional turning points are labeled rather than dramatized: "He was devastated." "She felt betrayed." The reader is informed about emotions rather than made to experience them. Telling replaces drama at the moments that matter most — not for economy or voice, but because the writer hasn't found their way into the scene.

**What to look for:** How are the scene's emotional peaks delivered — through sensory experience or through labels? When telling appears, is it purposeful or default? Does the prose redundantly label emotions already visible through action?

---

### avoid_adverbs

**When it works:** Verbs carry their own weight. "She shuffled" not "she walked slowly." "He snapped" not "he said angrily." When adverbs appear, they earn their place by contradicting expectation ("smiled coldly") or adding genuinely new information. The prose's energy comes from verbs and nouns.

**When it fails:** Adverbs prop up weak verbs throughout. Dialogue tags carry adverbs that duplicate the dialogue's emotion ("'I can't believe it!' she said incredulously"). The adverb tells the reader what the verb (or dialogue) already showed. The prose relies on modification rather than precision.

**What to look for:** Adverbs in dialogue tags. Adverbs modifying verbs that could be replaced by a single stronger verb. Whether adverbs create meaning or duplicate it.

---

### avoid_passive

**When it works:** Active voice drives the prose. Characters do things; things don't happen to them. When passive voice appears, it is deliberate: emphasizing the receiver ("The promotion was denied" — the faceless system is the point), creating helplessness, or obscuring agency for dramatic effect. Every passive construction is a choice.

**When it fails:** Passive voice is the default. "The door was opened. The room was entered. The letter was read." No one does anything; things just happen. The prose drains energy and obscures who is acting. The passive isn't creating an effect — it's a habit.

**What to look for:** Clusters of passive constructions. Whether passive voice serves a dramatic purpose (helplessness, institutional power, unknown agent) or is simply the writer's default. The energy level of action passages.

---

### write_what_you_know

**When it works:** The prose conveys sensory and emotional authenticity. Whether the material is from experience, research, or empathetic imagination, it feels lived-in — specific textures, particular smells, the emotional truth that makes a reader recognize an experience they may never have had. Characters in unfamiliar roles or cultures behave with specificity, not stereotype.

**When it fails:** Unfamiliar material is rendered in vague generalities or Hollywood cliche. A surgery scene sounds like a TV drama. A war scene reads as secondhand. Characters in unfamiliar cultures behave according to stereotype. Emotional experiences are abstract rather than grounded in physical and psychological specificity. The writer's research (or lack of it) is showing.

**What to look for:** Sensory details — do they feel observed or imagined from a stock of generic images? Character behavior in specialized settings — specific or stereotypical? Emotional rendering — grounded in the body or floating in abstraction?

---

### no_weather_dreams

**When it works:** Either the scene opens with character in action, conflict, or engagement (the rule followed), or it opens with weather/dream/waking that earns its place by doing triple duty: establishing atmosphere AND introducing character AND creating tension simultaneously. The opening is the best possible entrance into this scene.

**When it fails:** The scene opens with weather that delays conflict ("It was a beautiful spring morning"), a dream that is a cheap misdirect or transparent symbolism, or a waking-up sequence that is passive and uneventful ("She woke up and stretched"). The opening could be cut entirely without the reader losing anything.

**What to look for:** Does the scene open with weather, dream, or waking? If so, does the opening earn its place by serving multiple purposes, or does it delay the story? If not, does the opening engage the reader immediately?

---

### avoid_said_bookisms

**When it works:** "Said" is the default tag and is invisible. Many exchanges use action beats or context instead of tags. When alternatives appear, they carry information "said" cannot: "whispered" (volume), "lied" (irony). The reader is inside the conversation, never noticing attribution.

**When it fails:** Dialogue tags are a thesaurus exercise: "exclaimed," "declared," "queried," "opined," "retorted." Every tag calls attention to itself. The reader notices the attribution rather than the speech. The writer seems to believe "said" is boring.

**What to look for:** The variety of dialogue tags — are alternatives to "said" purposeful or habitual? Action beats replacing tags. Whether the reader would notice the attribution or not.

---

### kill_darlings

**When it works:** Every passage, no matter how beautiful, serves the story. Lyrical writing advances character, plot, or theme. The scene's length reflects its narrative importance. Nothing exists solely to showcase the writer's abilities. The prose is excellent because it is purposeful.

**When it fails:** The scene retains passages that serve the writer's pride rather than the story's needs. A three-page landscape description stops the narrative dead. A clever metaphor calls attention to the author's skill rather than the character's experience. The scene is longer than its narrative importance warrants because the writer was reluctant to cut material they loved.

**What to look for:** Passages that serve craft display rather than story. Ambitious writing that doesn't also advance character, plot, or theme. Material that could be cut without the reader noticing a gap. Scenes inflated beyond their narrative importance.

## 5-Point Scale Mapping

The diagnostic markers map to a 5-point scale as follows:

| Score | Label | Diagnostic Profile | Description |
|-------|-------|-------------------|-------------|
| **1** | Absent | All or nearly all markers indicate deficits | The principle is consistently violated or absent. No awareness of the technique. |
| **2** | Developing | Majority of markers indicate deficits, but some strength is present | The principle is present but inconsistent. Awareness without control. |
| **3** | Competent | Roughly even — some deficits, some strengths | The principle is functional. It mostly works with noticeable lapses. |
| **4** | Strong | Few deficits, most markers indicate strength | The principle is executed with confidence. Lapses are minor and rare. |
| **5** | Masterful | No deficits, or deficits are clearly intentional rule-breaking with artistic purpose | The technique is invisible, or broken deliberately to powerful effect. |

### Aggregation Formula

For each scene-principle pair:

```
deficit_points = sum(marker.weight for markers where answer matches deficit_if)
max_points = sum(all marker.weight)
deficit_ratio = deficit_points / max_points

Score mapping:
  deficit_ratio = 0.00        → 5 (Masterful)
  deficit_ratio ≤ 0.20        → 4 (Strong)
  deficit_ratio ≤ 0.50        → 3 (Competent)
  deficit_ratio ≤ 0.80        → 2 (Developing)
  deficit_ratio > 0.80        → 1 (Absent)
```

The thresholds are weighted by marker importance: a weight-2 marker finding a deficit shifts the score more than a weight-1 marker.
