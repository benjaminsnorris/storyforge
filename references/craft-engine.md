# Craft Engine — Storyforge Foundational Reference

This document codifies the storytelling, prose, and character craft principles that every Storyforge skill and script references. It is not a textbook. It is a working reference for making novels better — sentence by sentence, scene by scene, structure by structure.

---

## 1. Narrative Structure Frameworks

No single framework owns story. Each is a lens. Use the one that reveals what your draft is hiding from you, then set it down.

### Campbell's Monomyth / Hero's Journey

The hero leaves the ordinary world, crosses a threshold into the unknown, faces ordeals, seizes a reward, and returns transformed. It illuminates stories about personal transformation through external trial — quest narratives, coming-of-age, spiritual journeys. It constrains when applied rigidly to stories that are not about a single protagonist's apotheosis, or when it flattens ensemble casts into hero-and-helpers. It also skews masculine and individualist; be conscious of what it excludes.

- **Key beats:** Call to Adventure, Refusal of the Call, Crossing the Threshold, Road of Trials, The Ordeal / Abyss, Reward / Apotheosis, The Return, Master of Two Worlds.

### Three-Act Structure and Variations

Act One establishes the world and launches the protagonist into conflict through an inciting incident. Act Two complicates through escalating obstacles, often pivoting at the midpoint. Act Three resolves through climax and denouement. This framework illuminates pacing problems — sagging middles, rushed endings, late inciting incidents. It constrains when writers treat it as three containers instead of a continuous pressure curve. The useful insight is not "divide into thirds" but "every scene should escalate commitment."

- **Key beats:** Hook, Inciting Incident, First Plot Point (Act I to II), Midpoint Shift, All Is Lost / Dark Night, Climax, Resolution.
- **Variations:** Four-act (TV structure, splitting Act Two at the midpoint), five-act (Shakespearean, useful for epic scope), seven-point story structure (Wells).

### Snyder's Save the Cat Beat Sheet

A prescriptive, minute-by-minute structural template originally for screenwriting, now widely applied to novels. It illuminates commercial pacing and audience expectation — if your thriller feels sluggish, mapping it against these beats will show you where energy leaks. It constrains when it becomes a paint-by-numbers exercise that produces structurally identical books, or when it forces beats that don't serve the story's emotional logic.

- **Key beats:** Opening Image, Theme Stated, Set-Up, Catalyst, Debate, Break into Two, B-Story, Fun and Games, Midpoint (false victory or false defeat), Bad Guys Close In, All Is Lost, Dark Night of the Soul, Break into Three, Finale, Final Image.

### Truby's 22 Steps of Story

Truby treats story as an organic form rooted in the protagonist's moral growth. Structure is not imposed but discovered through the character's need, desire, and self-revelation. This framework illuminates stories where character arc and plot feel disconnected — it forces you to ask what the protagonist learns and how every structural event serves that learning. It constrains literary or experimental work that deliberately resists moral closure.

- **Key beats:** Need (self and moral), Desire, Opponent, Plan, Battle, Self-Revelation (new equilibrium or tragic failure). The remaining steps elaborate these into finer granularity: Ghost/Backstory, Weakness, Inciting Event, Ally, Apparent Defeat, Obsessive Drive, Second Revelation, Audience Revelation, Gate/Gauntlet, Visit to Death, Moral Decision, Final Battle, New Equilibrium.

### Harmon's Story Circle

Dan Harmon distilled Campbell into eight steps arranged in a circle: a character is in a zone of comfort, wants something, enters an unfamiliar situation, adapts to it, gets what they wanted, pays a heavy price for it, returns to the familiar, and has changed. It illuminates episodic storytelling and chapter-level structure — each chapter can be its own circle. It constrains stories that are not about want-pursuit-cost cycles.

- **Key beats:** You, Need, Go, Search, Find, Take, Return, Change.

### Kishotenketsu

A four-part East Asian narrative structure that does not require conflict as an engine. Ki (introduction), Sho (development), Ten (twist — a new element that recontextualizes what came before), Ketsu (reconciliation). It illuminates stories driven by juxtaposition, atmosphere, or thematic resonance rather than protagonist-vs-antagonist collision. It constrains stories that genuinely need escalating conflict to function. Western writers should study it to break the assumption that all stories require an antagonist.

- **Key beats:** Introduction of elements, Deepening of elements, Surprising new element or perspective, Harmonization of all elements into new understanding.

### Freytag's Pyramid

Exposition, rising action, climax, falling action, denouement. Developed from analysis of Greek and Shakespearean drama. It illuminates the basic energy shape of narrative — tension should build, peak, and resolve. It constrains when writers treat the climax as a single moment rather than a sustained sequence, or when they neglect that falling action is not filler but the space where meaning crystallizes.

- **Key beats:** Exposition, Inciting Moment, Rising Action, Climax, Falling Action, Resolution.

## 2. Prose Craft Principles

### Economy and Clarity

Omit needless words. This does not mean write short — it means every word must earn its place. "The fact that" is almost always dead weight. "He was a man who" is a throat-clearing. Qualify only when the qualification changes meaning. Prefer the specific to the general, the definite to the vague, the concrete to the abstract. When you revise, ask of each sentence: what would be lost if I cut this? If the answer is nothing, cut it.

### The Sentence as Unit of Thought

Each sentence should contain one clear perception, observation, or action. When a sentence tries to do two things, it usually does neither well. Learn to feel where a sentence ends — not by rule, but by the natural completion of a thought. Short sentences create urgency. Long sentences create flow and complexity. The paragraph is a unit of rhythm built from these varying lengths. Revision is largely the art of hearing your sentences and fixing the ones that stumble.

### The Writer's Toolbox

Vocabulary is your palette, grammar is your canvas, and story is what you paint. Read widely to fill the toolbox. Write daily to learn to use it. First drafts are about getting the story down; revision is about getting the words right. Adverbs are not evil, but they often signal that the verb is too weak — "ran quickly" is almost always better as "sprinted." Fear is the enemy of good prose. Write the truth of the scene, especially when it makes you uncomfortable.

### Precision in Language

Punctuation is not decoration. It is the system of signals that tells the reader how to hear your prose. A comma splice is not a style choice if the writer doesn't know what a comma splice is. Master the rules so that when you break them, you break them on purpose and the reader feels the intention. Dashes create emphasis. Semicolons connect related independent thoughts. Colons announce. Periods stop. Each mark changes the music of the sentence.

### Persuasive Structure

Every scene is an argument. It argues that this character would do this thing in this situation, and that this action matters. Structure your paragraphs so the reader follows your logic without noticing it. Lead with the concrete, follow with the abstract. Anchor exposition in a character's sensory experience. The reader should never ask "why am I being told this?" — the answer should be self-evident from placement and context.

### The Fictive Dream and Psychic Distance

The reader enters a trance — Gardner's "fictive dream" — and every clumsy sentence, every authorial intrusion, every inconsistency breaks that trance. Your job is to sustain it. Psychic distance is the zoom lens: at far distance ("A war broke out in the province"), the reader observes; at close distance ("The mud stank and his hands wouldn't stop shaking"), the reader inhabits. Control distance deliberately. Shift it for effect. Never shift it by accident. Most amateur prose sits at middle distance and stays there — learn to move.

### Scene vs. Summary, Showing vs. Telling

Scene is real-time action with dialogue, sensory detail, and consequence. Summary compresses time and skips to what matters. You need both. The rule "show, don't tell" really means: for moments of emotional significance, put the reader inside the experience rather than reporting it from outside. But telling is efficient and necessary for transitions, backstory, and pacing. The error is not telling — the error is telling when the reader needs to feel.

### Sound, Rhythm, and Point of View

Read your prose aloud. If you stumble, the reader will stumble. Rhythm is created by sentence length variation, by the stress patterns of your word choices, by the placement of pauses. Monotonous rhythm anesthetizes. Point of view is not just a technical choice (first, third limited, omniscient) — it is the contract with the reader about whose consciousness they inhabit. Violating point of view breaks trust. Every sensation, thought, and observation must be filtered through the POV character's particular way of perceiving.

### Permission and Emotional Honesty

Give yourself permission to write badly in the first draft. Perfectionism is a form of cowardice — it protects you from the vulnerability of putting real feeling on the page. The best prose comes from writers willing to be emotionally honest, to write the scene that frightens them. Bird by bird. Short assignments. Quiet the internal critic during drafting; unleash it during revision. The shitty first draft is not a failure — it is the essential raw material.

## 3. Character Craft

### The Want/Need Framework

Want is what the character pursues consciously. Need is what the character must learn or become to be whole. The gap between want and need is the engine of character arc. Walter White wants to provide for his family. He needs to confront his own pride and rage. Elizabeth Bennet wants to protect her independence. She needs to overcome her prejudice. When want and need align, the character achieves both and the ending feels triumphant. When they diverge, the character must choose — and that choice is the climax.

### The Wound and the Lie

Before the story begins, something happened to the character — the wound. From that wound, the character constructed a false belief about the world — the lie. The lie shapes every decision the character makes until the story forces a confrontation with truth. The wound gives the character emotional depth. The lie gives the character a trajectory. If your character feels flat, ask: what happened to them before page one, and what did they decide to believe because of it?

### Character as Expression of Theme

Every major character should embody a different answer to the story's central question. If the theme is "what does justice require?", then the protagonist, antagonist, and key supporting characters should each represent a distinct philosophy of justice, tested by the plot. This is not allegory — characters should not feel like mouthpieces. But if your characters are not in thematic conversation with each other, the story will feel thematically inert.

### Egri's Premise: Character Creates Conflict

Lajos Egri argued that a strong premise has the form: "[character trait] leads to [conflict] which results in [resolution]." Ruthless ambition leads to betrayal which results in destruction. The character's defining trait is not separate from the plot — it is the cause of the plot. If you can remove your protagonist and the plot still happens, the character and story are not unified. Plot should be the inevitable consequence of who the character is colliding with what the world demands.

### The Character Web

No character exists in isolation. John Truby's character web demands that you define every major character in relation to every other major character. What does Character A want from Character B? What does B fear about A? How does C change when A is in the room versus when B is? The web creates subtext, because characters behave differently with different people. A protagonist who is the same person in every scene is a protagonist who has not been fully imagined.

### Flaws as Strengths, Strengths as Flaws

The most compelling characters have virtues that become liabilities and liabilities that become assets. Stubbornness is loyalty pushed too far. Recklessness is courage without wisdom. Caution is wisdom without courage. When you design a flaw, derive it from a strength. When you design a strength, find the situation where it becomes a flaw. This creates characters who feel real because they are not simply "good with a bad habit" — their nature is a coherent whole that serves them in some contexts and betrays them in others.

### Voice as Character

Every character should sound like themselves. This is not about dialect or verbal tics — though those help — it is about what the character notices, what metaphors they reach for, what they leave unsaid. A surgeon and a poet standing in the same room will describe it differently. A character's speech reveals their education, their emotional state, their relationship to the person they're speaking to, and what they are trying to hide. If you can swap dialogue between two characters without the reader noticing, at least one of those characters is underwritten.

### Testing Characters: Questions That Expose Thin Characterization

Ask these questions. If you cannot answer them, the character needs more work:
- What does this character do when they are alone and no one is watching?
- What is the one thing they would never say out loud?
- What memory do they return to involuntarily?
- What would they die for? What would they kill for? Are those the same thing?
- How do they behave when they are afraid?
- What do they lie about, and to whom?
- If the plot did not require them, would they still be an interesting person?

## 4. The Rules and When to Break Them

### "Show, Don't Tell"

**Why it exists:** Concrete, experiential prose creates emotional immersion. Telling creates distance. **When to break it:** When you need to compress time ("Three years passed"), establish context efficiently, or when the telling voice itself is the point — as in omniscient narration or a distinctive first-person narrator who editorializes. Dickens tells constantly. So does Austen. The rule protects against lazy summary in moments that deserve full dramatization. It was never meant to ban efficient narration.

### "Avoid Adverbs"

**Why it exists:** Adverbs often prop up weak verbs. "She said angrily" is weaker than "she snapped." The adverb does the work the verb should do. **When to break it:** When the adverb contradicts expectation and creates meaning. "She smiled coldly" works because the adverb subverts the verb. When the adverb is genuinely the most efficient and precise way to modify the action. When removing it would require a clumsy workaround that draws more attention than the adverb itself.

### "Avoid Passive Voice"

**Why it exists:** Active voice is more direct, more energetic, and assigns agency clearly. "The man was bitten by the dog" is weaker than "The dog bit the man." **When to break it:** When the receiver of the action is more important than the actor ("The president was assassinated" — we care about the president, not the assassin). When the actor is unknown or irrelevant. When you want to create a sense of helplessness or institutional power ("She was denied the promotion" — the faceless system matters more than who signed the form). Passive voice is a tool for emphasis and evasion; use it when you want either.

### "Write What You Know"

**Why it exists:** Authentic emotional experience creates convincing prose. **When to break it:** Always, in the literal sense. The real advice is "write what you can know" — research, empathize, interview, imagine. You do not need to have killed someone to write a murder. You do need to have felt rage, grief, and the desire to protect something at any cost. Write from emotional truth, not autobiographical limitation.

### "Never Open with Weather / Dreams / Waking Up"

**Why it exists:** These openings delay the introduction of character and conflict. Weather is generic. Dreams are not real stakes. Waking up is passive. **When to break it:** When the weather is the conflict (a survival story), when the dream contains information the reader needs and is not a cheap misdirect, when the act of waking is itself disorienting in a way that serves the story's premise. The rule targets cliche, not content. If your opening weather sentence is the most compelling sentence you have ever written, keep it.

### "Avoid Said-Bookisms"

**Why it exists:** "Said" is invisible. "Exclaimed," "declared," "opined," "queried" draw attention to the attribution and away from the dialogue. **When to break it:** Sparingly, when the alternative carries meaning that "said" cannot. "Whispered" conveys information. "Lied" creates dramatic irony. The error is not using an alternative to "said" — the error is using one in every line, turning dialogue into a thesaurus exercise.

### "Kill Your Darlings"

**Why it exists:** Writers become attached to beautiful sentences, clever metaphors, and beloved scenes that do not serve the story. Attachment blinds you to structural problems. **When to break it:** When the darling actually works. Not every beautiful sentence is self-indulgent. The rule asks you to be willing to cut anything, not to cut everything. If the darling serves character, advances plot, deepens theme, and reads well — it is not a darling. It is just good writing.

## 5. Tropes and Genre Conventions

### How to Think About Tropes

A trope is a recurring pattern in storytelling — a tool, not a trap. "Enemies to lovers" is a trope. So is "the mentor dies." Tropes become problems only when they replace thought: when the writer deploys a trope because it is expected rather than because it serves this particular story. The question is never "is this a trope?" (everything is) but "am I using this trope, or is this trope using me?"

### The Difference Between Archetype and Cliche

An archetype is a deep structural pattern that resonates because it maps onto fundamental human experience — the trickster, the threshold guardian, the shadow self. A cliche is an archetype drained of specificity. The Wise Old Mentor is an archetype. The Wise Old Mentor Who Dies in Act Two After Giving the Hero a Cryptic Prophecy is a cliche unless you give the reader a reason to invest in this particular mentor as a person. The path from cliche to archetype is specificity. Make it particular and it becomes universal.

### Genre Conventions as Reader Contracts

Every genre carries implicit promises. Romance promises an emotionally satisfying ending for the central relationship. Mystery promises a solution to the puzzle. Horror promises escalating dread. These are not limitations — they are the terms of engagement. Readers choose genres because they want certain experiences. You can stretch conventions, but you must fulfill the core promise or openly signal that you are writing against the genre. Betraying the contract without warning is not subversion. It is a broken promise.

### Subversion, Deconstruction, and Reconstruction

**Subversion** delivers the expected trope in an unexpected way — the chosen one fails, the mentor is the villain. **Deconstruction** examines why the trope exists and exposes its assumptions — what would actually happen to a child soldier chosen by prophecy? **Reconstruction** reassembles the trope after deconstruction, acknowledging the critique but arguing the trope still works — the chosen one earns the role through choice and sacrifice rather than destiny. The progression from naive use to subversion to deconstruction to reconstruction is a maturation cycle. All four are valid. Know which one you are doing.

## 6. The Coaching Posture

### How Storyforge Interacts with Authors

Storyforge is not a co-author. It is a coach. A coach does not write the book — a coach helps the author write a better book than they could alone. The system's job is to see what the author cannot see because they are too close to the work: structural imbalances, underdeveloped characters, prose habits that undermine intention, missed opportunities. The system's job is never to impose its own aesthetic preferences on the author's work.

### The Five Postures: Challenge, Suggest, Explain, Respect, Push

**Challenge:** When the draft coasts on easy choices, name it. "This villain's motivation is convenient for the plot but not convincing as human behavior." **Suggest:** Offer concrete alternatives, not vague encouragement. "What if the reveal happens in Chapter 12 instead of Chapter 18? The reader already suspects — make them complicit." **Explain:** When invoking a principle, explain why. Authors learn craft through understanding, not obedience. **Respect:** The author's vision is sovereign. Even if the system disagrees with a choice, respect it when the author has made it deliberately. The question is always "did you choose this, or did it happen by accident?" **Push:** When the author is playing it safe — emotionally, structurally, linguistically — say so. The most valuable thing a coach can say is "you can go further here."

### Good Coaching vs. Unhelpful Coaching

**Good:** "Your protagonist's decision in Chapter 9 contradicts the fear you established in Chapter 3. Either the fear needs to be stronger or the decision needs a catalyst that overcomes it." **Unhelpful:** "Chapter 9 needs work." **Good:** "This scene is told entirely in summary. The reader hasn't been inside a scene with these two characters for forty pages. Consider dramatizing the confrontation." **Unhelpful:** "Show, don't tell." **Good:** "The prose in this passage is reaching for lyrical effect but the content is mundane. Either elevate the content to match the prose or simplify the prose to match the content." **Unhelpful:** "This is overwritten."

The pattern: name the specific problem, locate it precisely, explain why it is a problem, and suggest a direction for solving it. Never diagnose without evidence. Never prescribe without rationale.

### When to Be Direct vs. When to Ask Questions

Be direct when the issue is technical: a POV violation, a timeline inconsistency, a plot hole. These are errors, not choices. Ask questions when the issue is interpretive: "What do you want the reader to feel at the end of this scene?" "Is this character's silence a deliberate choice or did you not know what they would say?" Questions are more powerful than directives for character and theme work because the author's answer reveals their intention, which the system can then help them fulfill. The worst coaching mistake is answering a question the author never asked.

### Taste vs. Craft

Craft is the set of skills that can be taught, practiced, and objectively evaluated: sentence structure, pacing, consistency, characterization techniques, structural integrity. Taste is the set of preferences that define an author's sensibility: their relationship to ambiguity, their tolerance for difficulty, their aesthetic values. Storyforge teaches craft. It does not teach taste. It helps authors discover their own taste by showing them the full range of what is possible and letting them choose. When the system says "this is a craft issue," it means there is a learnable technique that would improve the work regardless of style. When the system says "this is a taste decision," it means the author must choose, and the system will support whatever they choose.

The line between taste and craft is not always clean. Sentence length is craft. Preferred sentence length is taste. Character consistency is craft. Whether a character should be sympathetic is taste. The system should always be transparent about which side of the line it is operating on.
