# Scoring Rubrics

## How to Use This Document

This document defines scoring criteria for the Storyforge principled scoring system. Every craft principle evaluated by the system has five score levels with concrete behavioral indicators and literary reference points. Evaluators should use these rubrics to anchor scores in observable textual evidence rather than subjective impression.

**Score levels:**
- **1 (Absent):** Principle consistently violated or missing. The manuscript shows no awareness of the technique.
- **2 (Developing):** Present but inconsistent. The writer demonstrates awareness without control.
- **3 (Competent):** Functional, mostly works. Execution is workmanlike with noticeable lapses. This is the median for a solid manuscript.
- **4 (Strong):** Executed with confidence and consistency. Lapses are minor and rare.
- **5 (Masterful):** Invisible technique, or deliberately broken for art. The principle is executed with such skill that it disappears into the reading experience.

**How exemplars work:** Each score level includes a brief literary reference illustrating that level of execution. These are drawn from published novels and recognized craft textbooks. They are reference points, not absolute standards -- a passage scoring 2 in one context might function differently in another.

## Scene Craft

These seven principles are scored per-scene.

### Enter Late, Leave Early

**1 (Absent):** Scenes open with extended preamble -- characters arriving, sitting down, exchanging pleasantries, ordering coffee -- before any meaningful action begins. Scenes end well past their turning point, with characters reflecting on what just happened, restating the scene's meaning, or narrating transitions to the next location. The reader must wade through setup to find the scene.
> The opening chapter of many early-draft manuscripts: the protagonist wakes up, showers, eats breakfast, drives to work, and arrives at the office before the first line of conflict appears. Contrast with the opening of Raymond Carver's "Cathedral," which begins mid-conversation with the narrator already bristling about his wife's blind friend coming to visit -- no preamble, no setup, straight into tension.

**2 (Developing):** Scenes begin within a paragraph or two of the essential action but still carry unnecessary orienting material. Endings occasionally linger past the turn, adding a reflective paragraph that dilutes impact. The writer knows the principle exists but has not yet developed the instinct for where to cut.
> A scene that opens with two sentences of a character entering a room before the confrontation begins -- functional but not yet sharp. The writer could cut those sentences and lose nothing.

**3 (Competent):** Scenes generally open close to the point of tension and end near the turn. The writer's instincts are developing -- most scenes start and stop in reasonable places. But there are noticeable lapses: an occasional scene that opens a beat too early or lingers a beat too long. The cuts are adequate rather than precise. A careful editor would trim a sentence here, a paragraph there.
> A scene that opens with one line of arrival before the conflict and closes with one reflective sentence after the turn. Neither is egregious, but both could be cut. The writer is close but has not yet internalized the ruthlessness the principle demands.

**4 (Strong):** Scenes open at or very near the point of tension. Endings land on or just after the turn, trusting the reader to carry the emotional weight forward. Occasional scenes open slightly early for deliberate pacing reasons (establishing calm before a storm), but these choices feel intentional rather than habitual.
> Hemingway's "Hills Like White Elephants" -- the story drops the reader into the middle of a conversation already in progress. No arrival, no setup. The tension is present from the first line: "The hills across the valley of the Ebro were long and white."

**5 (Masterful):** Every scene enters at the precise moment of maximum relevance and exits at the moment of maximum resonance. The cuts themselves become expressive -- what is omitted between scenes creates meaning. The reader never notices setup because there is none; the reader never notices an overstayed ending because the scene releases them at the peak of feeling.
> Cormac McCarthy's *No Country for Old Men* -- scenes begin mid-action and end mid-consequence. The novel's most significant death happens offscreen, between scenes. The cut itself is the point. Similarly, Denis Johnson's *Jesus' Son* cuts between scenes with a dreamlike precision that makes the gaps as meaningful as the text.

### Every Scene Must Turn

**1 (Absent):** Scenes exist as static tableaux -- characters talk, observe, or think without anything changing. No relationship shifts, no information is revealed, no commitment deepens or breaks. The scene could be removed without affecting the narrative. If pressed to state the scene's question and answer, neither can be articulated.
> McKee describes this in *Story* as the "non-event" -- a scene where characters exchange information the audience already has, or where emotional states remain exactly as they were at the opening. A dinner scene where characters catch up but nothing is at stake exemplifies a scene without a turn.

**2 (Developing):** The scene contains a turn, but it is minor, predictable, or muddled. The change may be present on the page but does not register emotionally. Alternatively, the scene attempts multiple turns and achieves none cleanly. The writer senses that scenes should change something but cannot yet execute turns that land.
> A scene where a character "decides to investigate" but the decision carries no weight because nothing in the scene pressured them toward or away from that choice. The turn exists logically but not dramatically.

**3 (Competent):** Scenes have identifiable turns -- something changes by the end. But the turns are often predictable, or their emotional impact is muted. The writer can construct a scene that moves from state A to state B, but the transition lacks surprise or force. The reader follows the logic without feeling the shift in their gut.
> A confrontation scene where two characters argue and one relents. The turn is real -- a value has changed -- but the outcome was visible from the scene's second paragraph. The machinery works; it just does not produce the jolt of a genuinely dramatic turn.

**4 (Strong):** Each scene has a clear, identifiable turn that changes values -- a relationship shifts, information reframes understanding, a plan succeeds or fails. The turn is earned by the scene's internal logic and felt by the reader. The scene's question and answer can be cleanly articulated.
> The party scene in *The Great Gatsby* (Chapter 3) -- Nick enters as an outsider, observes the spectacle, and finally meets Gatsby. The turn: Gatsby is not the larger-than-life figure the rumors promised but a surprisingly ordinary, even anxious man. Nick's understanding of the world he has entered shifts permanently.

**5 (Masterful):** The turn is both surprising and inevitable -- the reader did not see it coming but immediately recognizes it as the only possible outcome. Multiple values shift simultaneously (a character gains knowledge but loses trust). The turn reverberates beyond the scene, recontextualizing earlier events.
> The dinner scene in *Atonement* by Ian McEwan -- Briony witnesses an interaction between Cecilia and Robbie at the fountain. The scene turns on Briony's misinterpretation, and the turn does not merely change the scene's value; it sets the entire novel's tragedy in motion. Every element of the scene serves the turn while concealing it.

### Scene Emotion vs. Character Emotion

**1 (Absent):** Scene emotion and character emotion are always identical. When the character is happy, the scene feels happy. When the character is sad, the scene is melancholy. There is no gap between what the character feels and what the reader is made to feel. Dramatic irony, suspense, and tragic anticipation are absent. The reader has no access to information or feeling beyond what the POV character experiences.
> A first-person narrator describes being excited about a new job, and the scene conveys only excitement. No undercurrents, no signals to the reader that something might be wrong. The scene is emotionally one-dimensional.

**2 (Developing):** The writer occasionally creates a gap between character emotion and scene emotion, but it is inconsistent or heavy-handed. The reader may be told that they should feel differently from the character (through foreshadowing that announces itself) rather than being made to feel the gap through craft. The writer is aware of the concept but reaches for it clumsily.
> A scene where a character happily accepts an invitation, and the narrative voice inserts "she didn't know it would be the last time she saw him alive." The gap is created through direct authorial intervention rather than through the scene's own construction.

**3 (Competent):** The writer creates a gap between character emotion and scene emotion in some scenes and does so through scene construction rather than authorial announcement. But the gap is inconsistent -- present in key moments, absent in others where it would strengthen the work. When the gap appears, it generally works but lacks the sustained control that would make it powerful across an entire chapter or act.
> A scene where a character's cheerful preparations for a journey carry a faint undercurrent of dread because the reader knows what awaits. The gap works in this scene, but the preceding and following scenes have no such layering. The technique is available to the writer but not yet habitual.

**4 (Strong):** The writer controls the gap with skill. Scenes of calm carry undercurrents of dread when appropriate. Characters' blind spots are visible to the reader. The emotional experience of reading the scene is richer and more complex than the character's internal state. The gap is created through scene construction -- juxtaposition, detail selection, pacing -- rather than authorial announcement.
> In *Never Let Me Go* by Kazuo Ishiguro, Kathy narrates her school days with gentle nostalgia while the reader gradually realizes the horrifying truth about Hailsham. The gap between Kathy's fond tone and the reader's growing dread is sustained across the entire novel and is created entirely through carefully selected details, not authorial intrusion.

**5 (Masterful):** The gap between character emotion and scene emotion is the primary engine of the scene's power. The writer manipulates the reader's emotional experience with precision -- creating irony, suspense, pity, or anticipatory grief through the controlled distance between what the character knows/feels and what the reader knows/feels. The emotional layering is so integrated that it cannot be separated from the scene's other elements.
> The opening chapters of *Beloved* by Toni Morrison -- Sethe's matter-of-fact narration of daily life in a haunted house creates a devastating gap. Her numbness is the reader's horror. The scene emotion is grief and dread; the character emotion is exhausted endurance. The distance between them is the novel's emotional architecture. Similarly, the banquet scene in *Macbeth* -- Macbeth's terror at Banquo's ghost, invisible to the other characters, creates a gap where the audience experiences both his guilt and the court's bafflement simultaneously.

### Psychic Distance at Scene Level

**1 (Absent):** The scene maintains a single, flat psychic distance throughout -- usually middle distance. There is no zoom from establishing shot to close-up. The scene's climactic moment is narrated at the same distance as its setup. Alternatively, the distance lurches between extremes without pattern or purpose, pulling the reader in and pushing them away randomly.
> "Sarah walked into the room. She was nervous. The room was large and had many windows. She sat down. She thought about what she would say. The man across the table looked at her." -- Every sentence sits at the same remove. No approach, no deepening.

**2 (Developing):** The writer shows awareness of psychic distance and attempts to move closer during intense moments, but the transitions are abrupt or mechanical. The writer may default to a comfortable distance and only occasionally break out of it. The writer knows distance should shift but cannot yet control the shift smoothly.
> A scene that narrates in steady third-person summary and then suddenly drops into present-tense interiority for one paragraph before snapping back. The shift is visible as technique rather than experienced as immersion.

**3 (Competent):** Distance narrows during the scene's most important moments and widens for transitions. The writer has a functional sense of when to move closer and when to pull back. But the transitions are sometimes too abrupt or too slow, and the distance does not always match the emotional stakes. The climactic moment usually gets closer distance, but the approach is workmanlike rather than artful.
> A scene where the establishing frame is at middle distance, the confrontation moves to close distance, and the aftermath pulls back. The arc is present and basically correct, but the transitions feel like gear changes rather than a continuous zoom. The reader notices the technique rather than simply feeling drawn in.

**4 (Strong):** Distance narrows deliberately as the scene builds. The establishing frame is wide enough to orient the reader, the approach is gradual, and the scene's turning moment is rendered at close distance. The pull-back after the turn is measured, not abrupt. The reader feels drawn into the scene rather than observing it.
> Gardner describes the ideal distance arc in *The Art of Fiction*: "It was winter of the year 1853. A large man stepped out of a doorway. Henry J. Warburton had never much cared for snowstorms. Snow. Henry hated it." -- Each sentence steps closer, from panoramic to intimate, demonstrating controlled approach.

**5 (Masterful):** Psychic distance is used as an expressive instrument. The distance arc mirrors the emotional arc so precisely that the reader does not notice the technique -- they simply feel drawn in and released. Distance shifts serve characterization (a dissociating character creates involuntary distance shifts), theme (a novel about alienation maintains unusual distance), or structural purpose (a scene holds maximum closeness through the turn, making the reader feel the impact physically).
> Virginia Woolf's *Mrs Dalloway* -- the psychic distance shifts fluidly between characters and between close interiority and urban panorama, with each shift serving the novel's themes of connection and isolation. The reader moves from inside Clarissa's sensory experience ("What a lark! What a plunge!") to the wide view of London without ever feeling a seam. Similarly, the final paragraphs of *The Dead* by James Joyce -- the psychic distance slowly widens from Gabriel's specific grief to a vast, almost cosmic perspective as snow falls across Ireland.

### Show vs. Tell in Scenes

**1 (Absent):** The scene's pivotal moments are summarized rather than dramatized. Emotional turning points are reported ("She was devastated") rather than rendered through action, dialogue, and sensory detail. The reader is told what to feel instead of being given the experience that produces feeling. Conversely, mundane transitions that should be told are dramatized at unnecessary length.
> "They had a terrible argument about the money. She was furious and he was defensive. Eventually she stormed out." -- The most dramatic moment in the scene is compressed into summary. The reader is locked out of the experience. As Gardner writes in *The Art of Fiction*: "In bad or unsatisfying fiction, this authorial filtering keeps us from the characters' experience."

**2 (Developing):** Key moments are partially shown -- there is dialogue and some action -- but the writer supplements with emotional labels that undercut the showing. "I hate you," she said angrily. The anger is in the dialogue; the adverb tells us what we already know. The writer knows showing matters but reflexively explains what they have just shown.
> A scene that dramatizes a character walking across town in real time but then summarizes the conversation at their destination. The proportion of showing to telling is inverted.

**3 (Competent):** Pivotal moments are shown more often than not, and the writer generally avoids labeling emotions that are already visible in the scene. But the calibration between showing and telling is imperfect -- some important moments get insufficient scene treatment, while some transitions are dramatized longer than necessary. The writer's instincts are sound but not yet precise.
> A scene where the confrontation is dramatized with dialogue and action, but the writer still inserts a telling sentence at the peak moment: "He realized she would never forgive him." The showing was doing the work; the telling sentence is a safety net the writer has not yet learned to remove.

**4 (Strong):** Pivotal moments are fully dramatized with sensory specificity. The reader experiences the turning point in real time. Telling is used efficiently for transitions, backstory, and time compression -- it serves pacing rather than replacing drama. The writer rarely labels emotions that are already visible through action and dialogue.
> Le Guin notes in *Steering the Craft*: "The incredible power of the specific detail to convince, to move, to lead the imagination." A strong scene shows the character's hands shaking around a coffee cup rather than stating "he was nervous."

**5 (Masterful):** The balance between showing and telling is so calibrated that the reader never notices the technique. Shown moments land with full sensory and emotional impact. Told passages compress time with elegance and economy. The writer knows precisely which moments the reader needs to inhabit and which can be traversed. Telling, when it appears, has the authority of a narrator the reader trusts completely.
> The opening of Flannery O'Connor's "A Good Man Is Hard to Find" -- the grandmother's character is shown through her actions (sneaking the cat into the car, dressing so she'll be identifiable as a lady if there's an accident) rather than described through adjectives. Every detail tells us who she is without the narrator ever saying it. At the novel level, Morrison's *Beloved* interweaves shown memory-scenes with told history in a way that makes the telling as visceral as the showing -- "Sethe knew that the circle she was making around the room, the table, the stove could not be broken" gives us interiority through action.

### Thread Management

**1 (Absent):** Threads are introduced and then forgotten for long stretches, with no anchoring when they return. Alternatively, a single thread dominates every scene, becoming monotonous. Scenes feel disconnected from the larger narrative web -- individual scenes may work in isolation but do not contribute to the sense of an interconnected story. The reader cannot track what is important because the narrative does not signal it.
> A subplot about a character's estranged sibling is introduced in Chapter 2, disappears entirely until Chapter 14, and returns with no sensory or emotional link to its last appearance. The reader has to work to remember what it was about.

**2 (Developing):** Threads are tracked but managed mechanically. A scene touches its assigned threads but without organic integration -- the writer seems to be checking boxes. Thread reintroduction feels forced rather than natural. The writer is aware that threads need management but treats it as a bookkeeping task rather than a craft.
> A scene where a character pauses mid-conversation to think about three separate subplots in sequence, each getting a paragraph of reflection. The threads are present but feel like interruptions rather than woven elements.

**3 (Competent):** Threads are managed with reasonable skill. Each scene advances at least one thread and touches others. Thread reintroduction is usually organic rather than forced. But the weaving lacks subtlety -- threads tend to surface through direct character thought rather than through subtext, environmental detail, or action. The reader can follow the narrative web, but the connections are visible as craft rather than felt as accumulated meaning.
> A scene that advances the main plot thread through dialogue and touches a secondary thread through a brief interior reflection. The thread management is functional and the reader stays oriented, but the connections feel like they are being maintained rather than deepened. A dormant thread returns through a character remembering it rather than through a sensory or emotional echo.

**4 (Strong):** Each scene advances one to two threads while touching others lightly through subtext, environmental detail, or brief character thought. Dormant threads resurface with sensory anchors that connect to their last appearance. The reader feels the narrative accumulating meaning across scenes. No scene feels disconnected from the whole.
> In *The Remains of the Day* by Kazuo Ishiguro, the thread of Stevens's feelings for Miss Kenton weaves through scenes that are ostensibly about other things -- his professional duties, his journey, his memories of Lord Darlington. The thread surfaces through telling details (polishing silver she once touched, remembering exactly where she stood) rather than direct statement.

**5 (Masterful):** Threads weave with the intricacy of a fugue. A gesture in Chapter 3 echoes in Chapter 18 and the reader feels the connection viscerally. Touching a dormant thread creates the feeling of the novel "remembering itself." The number of threads per scene is precisely calibrated -- never so few that the scene feels isolated, never so many that it loses its center. Thread convergence at key moments produces the sense of inevitability that distinguishes great novels from good ones.
> *Middlemarch* by George Eliot -- dozens of character threads, social threads, and thematic threads weave through hundreds of scenes, and yet the novel never loses track. When threads converge (Dorothea's discovery of Rosamond with Ladislaw, for instance), the convergence feels like the entire novel has been building to this moment. Similarly, *A Visit from the Goon Squad* by Jennifer Egan manages threads across decades and perspectives, with objects and phrases recurring in ways that make the fragmented structure feel like a unified whole.

### Pacing Through Scene Variety

**1 (Absent):** Scenes are monotonous in type, length, or intensity. Five consecutive high-tension scenes with no respite, or five consecutive quiet dialogue scenes with no escalation. Scene length is uniform, producing a metronomic rhythm that flattens emotional impact. The writer has one gear and stays in it.
> A thriller manuscript where every chapter is a chase or confrontation at roughly the same intensity level. By Chapter 10, the reader is numb to danger because danger is the default state. As McKee notes in *Story*: "If every scene screams with high drama, the audience goes deaf."

**2 (Developing):** Some variety exists, but it feels accidental rather than designed. The writer alternates between scene types but without a sense of strategic rhythm. A quiet scene may follow an action scene, but the quiet scene does not deepen the meaning of what preceded it or build anticipation for what follows. The writer is aware that sameness is a problem but addresses it by alternating rather than by orchestrating.
> A novel that alternates between dialogue and action scenes in a simple A-B-A-B pattern. The variation is present but mechanical, like a metronome switching between two tempos rather than an orchestra modulating dynamics.

**3 (Competent):** Scene variety is present and generally serves the pacing. The writer alternates intensity levels, varies scene lengths, and mixes scene types with reasonable skill. But the rhythm is more functional than expressive -- scenes of different types follow each other in a sensible order without creating the feeling of strategic orchestration. The pacing works but does not create the sense of being carried by the narrative's momentum.
> A novel where high-tension scenes are followed by quieter scenes, and the reader gets necessary breathing room. Scene lengths vary. But the variation feels like good instinct rather than precise design -- a musician playing in time without playing with dynamics. The pacing is solid but not a source of power in itself.

**4 (Strong):** Scene variety is deliberate and effective. High-intensity scenes are followed by scenes that allow the reader to process and deepen their understanding. Short, sharp scenes create acceleration when needed. Long, immersive scenes create weight. The writer uses scene type, length, and intensity as pacing instruments. The reader feels the rhythm of the novel and is carried by it.
> *The Road* by Cormac McCarthy alternates between brief, harrowing encounters and longer passages of quiet endurance. The short scenes hit harder because of the silence surrounding them. The long passages earn their length because the reader needs the breathing room.

**5 (Masterful):** Scene variety is orchestrated with the precision of a musical composition. The rhythm of scene types creates meaning -- acceleration through compressed scenes before a climax, deceleration through expansive scenes during aftermath. Scene length itself communicates: a one-page scene amid ten-page scenes shocks; a thirty-page scene amid five-page scenes creates gravitational pull. The pacing is so integral to the reading experience that changing the scene order would fundamentally alter the novel's emotional impact.
> *Beloved* by Toni Morrison -- the novel's non-linear structure juxtaposes scene types and temporal frames to create a rhythm that mimics memory itself: short, sharp flashbacks interrupt longer present-day scenes, building in intensity until the full horror surfaces. The pacing is the novel's psychology made structural. Similarly, *2666* by Roberto Bolano -- the "Part About the Crimes" uses relentless, brief, almost clinical scene repetition to create a cumulative dread that no single scene could achieve.

## Prose Craft

These nine principles are scored per-scene.

### Economy and Clarity

**1 (Absent):** Prose is cluttered with needless words, redundancies, and throat-clearing phrases. "The fact that," "it was clear that," "he was a man who," "in order to" appear regularly. Sentences contain qualifications that add nothing. Descriptions are vague and general where they should be specific and concrete. Cutting 30% of the words would lose no meaning.
> "She was someone who always seemed to have a tendency to find herself in situations where she felt somewhat uncomfortable in social settings." This sentence has one idea buried under twelve unnecessary words. Strunk & White's *The Elements of Style* addresses this directly: "Omit needless words. Vigorous writing is concise."

**2 (Developing):** Prose is mostly clean but occasionally bloated. The writer defaults to extra qualifiers ("somewhat," "rather," "slightly") and hedging language. Some sentences are tight and effective; others carry dead weight. The writer understands economy in principle but has not yet made it habitual.
> Functional prose that communicates clearly but lacks the compression that makes sentences memorable. "He walked across the room and sat down in the chair near the window" -- nothing is wrong, but "He sat by the window" does the same work in five words.

**3 (Competent):** Prose is generally efficient. Most sentences earn their length, and obvious bloat has been edited out. But a careful reader still finds hedging qualifiers, occasional redundancies, and sentences that could be tightened without loss. The writer revises for economy but has not yet achieved the instinct for it -- the discipline shows as effort rather than as second nature.
> Prose where most paragraphs are clean but one or two sentences per page carry a word or phrase that adds nothing. "She quickly realized that the situation was becoming somewhat dangerous" -- "quickly" and "somewhat" pull in opposite directions and neither earns its place. The sentence works; it just is not as sharp as it could be.

**4 (Strong):** Every sentence earns its place. Needless words are rare. The writer chooses the specific over the general, the concrete over the abstract. Descriptions are precise. The prose communicates efficiently without feeling stripped or austere (unless austerity is the chosen style). Revision has clearly occurred -- the prose shows the discipline of cutting.
> Hemingway's *The Old Man and the Sea*: "He was an old man who fished alone in a skiff in the Gulf Stream and he had gone eighty-four days now without taking a fish." Every word carries weight. No word can be removed without loss. This is economy in service of clarity, not minimalism for its own sake.

**5 (Masterful):** Prose achieves maximum meaning in minimum space. Each word is precisely chosen. Sentences are compressed to their essential force without sacrificing rhythm or beauty. The economy is so thorough that adding a word would weaken the sentence rather than strengthen it. The prose feels inevitable -- as if it could not have been written any other way.
> "The cold passed reluctantly from the earth, and the retiring fogs revealed an army stretched out on the hills, resting." -- Stephen Crane, *The Red Badge of Courage*. Three things happen in one sentence: weather, revelation, and the positioning of an entire army. Nothing is wasted. Similarly, Le Guin's prose in *The Left Hand of Darkness*: "The king was pregnant" -- four words that reframe an entire world.

### The Sentence as Unit of Thought

**1 (Absent):** Sentences routinely try to accomplish two or three things at once, resulting in confusion. Run-on constructions pile clause upon clause without a sense of where one thought ends and another begins. Alternatively, all sentences are the same length and structure, creating a monotonous rhythm that anesthetizes the reader. Paragraphs feel like blocks of undifferentiated text rather than composed units.
> "He walked into the room and saw her sitting there and she was reading a book and it was the same book he had given her for her birthday which was in June and they had gone to the lake where his parents had a cabin." -- One sentence doing the work of five, with no sense of which detail matters most.

**2 (Developing):** Sentences generally contain one thought each, but rhythm is flat. The writer defaults to a single sentence length and structure (subject-verb-object, twelve to fifteen words). Paragraph construction is functional but not rhythmic. The writer has not yet learned to hear their sentences as units of music.
> Competent narrative prose with consistent medium-length sentences. Nothing stumbles, but nothing sings either. The writer follows the rules of grammar without exploiting the expressive possibilities of sentence variation.

**3 (Competent):** Sentences carry one thought each and some variation in length and structure is present. The writer occasionally uses a short sentence for emphasis or a longer sentence for flow. But the variation feels deliberate in spots and absent in others -- the writer can hear the rhythm when they concentrate but does not sustain it. Paragraphs have basic shape but lack the internal architecture of a composed unit.
> Prose where a punchy short sentence appears after a run of medium-length sentences and the effect works -- but then the next three paragraphs revert to uniform sentence length. The tool is in the toolbox; it just is not yet used consistently.

**4 (Strong):** Each sentence carries one clear thought, and sentence length varies to create rhythm. Short sentences punch. Long sentences flow. Paragraphs have internal shape -- they build, crest, and resolve. The prose reads smoothly aloud. The writer uses sentence structure expressively: fragments for emphasis, complex sentences for nuance, parallel structures for force.
> "He did not know how long he had been in the water. He only knew the light was going. And the light was going fast." -- A strong passage uses sentence variation to mirror the character's mental state: the first sentence is measured; the fragments that follow accelerate as panic sets in.

**5 (Masterful):** Sentences function as units of music and thought simultaneously. Each sentence completes precisely one perception, observation, or action, and the way it completes it -- its length, its rhythm, its internal cadence -- mirrors the content. The paragraph as a composed unit of rhythm is fully realized. Reading the prose aloud reveals not just clarity but beauty -- the sentences have been heard, not merely written.
> Virginia Woolf, *To the Lighthouse*: "For now she need not think about anybody. She could be herself, by herself. And that was what now she often felt the need of -- to think; well, not even to think. To be silent; to be alone." Each sentence narrows, slows, and quiets, mirroring Mrs. Ramsay's withdrawal into solitude. The paragraph's rhythm is its meaning. Similarly, the closing paragraph of *The Great Gatsby*: "So we beat on, boats against the current, borne back ceaselessly into the past." -- A single sentence that contains an entire novel's worth of thematic weight, carried by its rhythm.

### The Writer's Toolbox

**1 (Absent):** The writer's vocabulary is limited and repetitive. The same words and phrases recur across scenes. Verbs are weak and propped up by adverbs. Metaphors, when attempted, are cliched or mixed. The writer shows no evidence of wide reading informing their prose choices. Grammar is treated as an obstacle rather than a tool.
> "She ran quickly to the door. She opened it quickly. The man outside looked angry. He was very tall." -- Limited vocabulary, weak verbs, no figurative language, no sense that the writer has resources beyond basic declarative sentences.

**2 (Developing):** The writer has a reasonable vocabulary and attempts figurative language, but metaphors are sometimes strained or cliched. Verb choices are sometimes precise, sometimes generic. The writer is aware of tools like metaphor, simile, and imagery but deploys them inconsistently -- reaching for effects without reliably achieving them.
> Prose that reaches for lyrical effects but overreaches: "The sunset was like a painting, the clouds a canvas of red and gold." The impulse is right but the execution relies on the first comparison that came to mind rather than finding the precise image.

**3 (Competent):** The writer draws from a solid vocabulary and uses figurative language that generally works. Verb choices are more precise than generic. Metaphors are functional and occasionally fresh. But the writer's range is visible -- they have a comfort zone of techniques they execute well and territory beyond it where they are less sure. The toolbox is stocked but not yet fully mastered.
> Prose with good verb choices and the occasional metaphor that lands cleanly, but where the writer defaults to familiar patterns in passages that demand something new. The similes work but do not surprise. The vocabulary is adequate but the writer rarely finds the word that makes the reader stop and feel something shift.

**4 (Strong):** The writer draws from a rich vocabulary without becoming showy. Verbs are precise and carry the weight that adverbs would otherwise bear. Metaphors are fresh and purposeful. The writer's reading life is visible in their prose -- they have internalized a range of techniques and deploy them with confidence. Grammar is used flexibly and correctly.
> Le Guin in *Steering the Craft* describes this level: "The skilled writer uses the whole orchestra -- not just the drums, not just the strings, but all the sections together, in whatever combination the passage requires."

**5 (Masterful):** The full range of the writer's tools is deployed with precision and apparent effortlessness. Vocabulary is wide but never showy -- the right word appears because the writer knows enough words to find it. Metaphors are striking, original, and organic to the material. The writer can shift registers -- from plain to lyrical, from sparse to dense -- as the scene demands. Technique is invisible; only effect is visible.
> Toni Morrison, *Beloved*: "124 was spiteful. Full of a baby's venom." Two sentences that demonstrate total command -- precise verbs, startling juxtaposition (a house that is spiteful, a baby that has venom), economy, and rhythm. The toolbox is full and every tool has been mastered. Similarly, McCarthy's prose in *Blood Meridian* draws on an archaic, biblical register with scientific precision: "They rode on and the sun in the east flushed the whole underside of the slate overcast and then the snow was pink and then there was a sunrise briefly and then it snowed again."

### Precision in Language

**1 (Absent):** Punctuation is inconsistent or incorrect in ways that obscure meaning. Comma splices, missing commas, and misused semicolons are frequent and appear accidental rather than intentional. The writer does not distinguish between dashes and hyphens, or between semicolons and colons. The reader must re-read sentences to parse them correctly.
> "She knew the answer, she had known it all along; but she couldn't say it: because." -- Punctuation used randomly, without understanding of what each mark does. The sentence is incoherent not because of its ideas but because of its signals.

**2 (Developing):** Punctuation is mostly correct but unadventurous. The writer relies on periods and commas, rarely using dashes, semicolons, or colons for effect. When they do attempt them, the usage is sometimes incorrect or uncertain. The writer treats punctuation as grammar rather than as music.
> Competent but flat: "She knew the answer. She had known it all along. But she couldn't say it." -- Grammatically correct, but the writer has not considered whether different punctuation choices might change the sentence's music and meaning.

**3 (Competent):** Punctuation is correct and the writer occasionally uses dashes, semicolons, or colons for effect. The attempts generally work, but the writer does not use the full punctuation toolkit with confidence. Some passages show purposeful punctuation choices; others default to safe constructions. The writer is developing a sense of punctuation as expressive, not merely mechanical.
> Prose where a well-placed dash creates emphasis in one paragraph, but the next three paragraphs use only periods and commas. The writer can use punctuation expressively but does not yet do so as a matter of habit. The moments of purposeful punctuation work; they are just infrequent.

**4 (Strong):** Punctuation is correct and purposeful. Dashes create emphasis. Semicolons connect related thoughts. Colons announce and deliver. The writer uses the full punctuation toolkit and each mark serves the sentence's meaning and rhythm. When rules are broken, the breaking feels deliberate.
> "She knew the answer -- had known it all along -- but she couldn't say it." The dashes create a parenthetical that feels like a rush of realization, different from what commas or parentheses would produce. The writer is using punctuation to control how the reader hears the sentence.

**5 (Masterful):** Punctuation is an expressive instrument fully integrated with prose rhythm. The writer controls pacing, emphasis, and connection at the level of individual marks. Rule-breaking is clearly intentional and creates effects that rule-following could not achieve. The reader does not notice the punctuation -- they simply hear the prose correctly.
> Emily Dickinson's dashes create a rhythm and ambiguity that commas or periods could not. Cormac McCarthy's deliberate omission of quotation marks in dialogue creates a flowing, dreamlike quality where speech and narration merge. Jose Saramago's extended sentences with comma-separated dialogue create a breathless, communal voice. Each writer's punctuation choices are inseparable from their prose's identity.

### Persuasive Structure

**1 (Absent):** Paragraphs and scenes lack internal logic. The reader frequently wonders why they are being told something at this point in the narrative. Exposition appears in blocks unconnected to character experience. Backstory is dumped rather than woven. The reader is aware of being informed rather than experiencing a story.
> A paragraph of world-building inserted between two lines of dialogue, with no character motivation for the information. "The Kingdom of Aldoria had been founded three hundred years ago by..." -- the reader is pulled out of the scene to receive a history lesson. As McKee writes: "Exposition is ammunition. It must be fired at the target, not stored in the warehouse."

**2 (Developing):** Paragraphs generally follow a logical order, but exposition is sometimes awkwardly placed. The writer knows to anchor information in character experience but does not always succeed. Some passages feel like detours. The writer is aware of the principle but execution is uneven.
> A scene where a character's backstory is triggered by a relevant stimulus but then extends for two paragraphs beyond what the moment justifies. The entry into exposition is organic; the exit is not.

**3 (Competent):** Paragraphs are logically ordered and exposition is mostly woven into scenes through character perception or dialogue. The reader rarely asks "why am I being told this?" But the weaving is sometimes visible as technique -- backstory arrives at sensible moments without achieving the feeling of inevitability. The information architecture is sound but the reader occasionally senses the writer making decisions about placement rather than experiencing the flow as natural.
> A scene where a character notices a photograph and remembers a relevant piece of backstory. The trigger is organic and the information is needed, but the transition into and out of the memory is slightly mechanical. The reader accepts it but does not feel that the information could only have arrived at this exact moment.

**4 (Strong):** Every paragraph earns its placement. Exposition is woven into scenes through character perception, dialogue, or action. The reader never asks "why am I being told this?" because the answer is self-evident from context. Backstory arrives when the reader wants it, not when the writer wants to deliver it. The argument of each scene -- why this character would do this thing in this situation -- is convincing.
> Francine Prose in *Reading Like a Writer* praises the way Chekhov delivers exposition: "Everything we need to know is communicated through what the characters do and say, and through the objects that surround them." A well-structured scene reveals its world through inhabiting it.

**5 (Masterful):** Information arrives with such apparent inevitability that the reader cannot imagine it placed elsewhere. The structuring of paragraphs within scenes creates an invisible argument that the reader follows without noticing. Exposition feels like revelation rather than instruction. The reader is never ahead of the scene (bored) or behind it (confused) -- they are precisely where the writer wants them to be.
> The opening of *One Hundred Years of Solitude* by Gabriel Garcia Marquez: "Many years later, as he faced the firing squad, Colonel Aureliano Buendia was to remember that distant afternoon when his father took him to discover ice." Past, present, and future are fused in a single sentence. The reader is given three temporal frames and accepts all of them because the sentence's structure is so persuasive. No exposition is dumped; everything is revealed through the architecture of the sentence itself.

### The Fictive Dream and Psychic Distance

**1 (Absent):** The fictive dream is broken regularly. Authorial intrusions, inconsistencies, anachronisms, and clumsy phrasing pull the reader out of the narrative. The prose calls attention to itself as writing rather than functioning as a window into the story. Psychic distance sits at a flat middle range and never moves. The reader observes the story rather than inhabiting it.
> Gardner in *The Art of Fiction*: "The writer who cannot distinguish between what a character would notice and what the writer notices is the writer who breaks the dream." A medieval character who thinks in modern idioms, a third-person narrator who suddenly reveals information the POV character cannot know, a metaphor so labored that the reader thinks about the author rather than the character.

**2 (Developing):** The dream is sustained in stretches but broken intermittently. The writer is capable of immersive passages but has not learned to sustain them. Psychic distance shifts but sometimes shifts by accident -- the narrator slips into a character's thoughts without the reader being prepared. The writer can create the dream but cannot yet protect it consistently.
> A passage that draws the reader deep into a character's sensory experience and then breaks the spell with an awkward simile or an observation the character would not plausibly make. The writer can create the dream but cannot yet protect it.

**3 (Competent):** The fictive dream is sustained through most of the scene. Disruptions are infrequent and minor -- a slightly off word choice, a briefly uncertain psychic distance shift. The reader stays inside the story for long stretches. But the dream lacks the depth and totality that would make the reader forget they are reading. The prose is transparent enough not to intrude but not so transparent that it disappears.
> A scene where the reader is immersed for several pages but is pulled out briefly by a metaphor that feels more like the author than the character, or by a moment where the psychic distance wobbles. The dream is maintained but not deepened -- the reader is inside the story without being lost in it.

**4 (Strong):** The fictive dream is sustained throughout. Psychic distance shifts are deliberate and controlled. The reader inhabits the POV character's consciousness without disruption. Prose is transparent -- it does not call attention to itself (unless opacity is the deliberate style). The world of the story feels real and internally consistent.
> The sustained close third person of *Olive Kitteridge* by Elizabeth Strout -- the reader is inside Olive's particular, prickly way of seeing the world for the entire book. Every observation is filtered through her consciousness. The dream is unbroken because the voice is consistent.

**5 (Masterful):** The fictive dream is not merely sustained but deepened -- the reader forgets they are reading. Psychic distance is an expressive tool used with precision, creating effects that range from intimate identification to cosmic perspective. The prose is so transparent (or so deliberately opaque, if that is the style) that technique disappears entirely. The reader does not admire the writing; the reader lives inside the world.
> *The Road* by McCarthy -- the reader inhabits the father's consciousness so completely that the world's ash-covered desolation becomes sensory reality. The prose is spare, unadorned, and perfectly transparent. The dream is total. Similarly, *Mrs Dalloway* achieves a different kind of total dream -- the prose is ornate and the psychic distance shifts constantly, but the reader never falls out of the experience because every shift is controlled and purposeful.

### Scene vs. Summary, Showing vs. Telling

**1 (Absent):** The writer defaults to either all-scene or all-summary without strategic purpose. Critical emotional moments are summarized while trivial transitions are dramatized in full. The writer does not distinguish between moments that deserve scene treatment and moments that should be compressed. When showing, the prose labels emotions rather than rendering them; when telling, the prose is flat and uninformative.
> "The next three months were terrible. She cried a lot and couldn't eat. Then one day she decided to move on." -- Three months of emotional devastation compressed into three sentences, while an earlier scene about grocery shopping occupied two pages. The proportion is inverted.

**2 (Developing):** The writer can identify important moments and gives them scene treatment, but the execution is uneven. Showing is present but supplemented with emotional labels ("she felt sad as she watched him leave"). Summary is used for transitions but sometimes compresses material that deserves dramatization. The writer knows the difference but cannot yet calibrate the balance.
> A scene that dramatizes a confrontation effectively but begins with a paragraph of told emotion: "She had been dreading this conversation all week. She felt anxious and unprepared." The showing works; the telling that frames it undermines the impact.

**3 (Competent):** Scene and summary are deployed with reasonable judgment. Important moments are usually shown; transitions are usually summarized. The calibration is mostly right, with occasional missteps -- a moment that deserved more scene treatment gets compressed, or a transition gets slightly more dramatization than it needs. Telling, when it appears, is functional rather than authoritative.
> A chapter where the three most important moments are shown in full and the connective tissue is summarized. The balance works, but one pivotal conversation is rendered in half-scene (some dialogue, some summary) when full dramatization would have served it better. The writer's judgment is sound but not yet precise.

**4 (Strong):** Scene and summary are deployed strategically. Pivotal moments are shown in full sensory detail. Transitions and time compressions are summarized efficiently. The reader experiences emotional turning points firsthand and accepts summary for everything else. Telling, when it appears, is authoritative and purposeful rather than lazy.
> "Three years passed" is an efficient and necessary summary when those three years are not the story. The reader trusts the narrator to show them what matters and tell them the rest. Ishiguro's *The Remains of the Day* exemplifies this: long scenic passages for emotional turning points, efficient summary for the journey between them.

**5 (Masterful):** The balance between scene and summary is invisible. The writer moves between modes with such fluency that the reader does not notice the transitions. Summary passages carry the authority and specificity of scene; scene passages contain the compression and efficiency of summary. The writer has internalized the principle so thoroughly that the question of when to show and when to tell answers itself.
> *Beloved* moves between scene and summary, between present and past, between showing and telling, with a fluency that makes the categories dissolve. "She just flew. Collected every bit of life she had made, all the parts of her that were precious and fine and beautiful, and carried, pushed, dragged them through the veil, out, away, over there where no one could hurt them." This is simultaneously showing and telling, scene and summary -- it is pure narration that has the vividness of dramatization.

### Sound, Rhythm, and Point of View

**1 (Absent):** Prose does not read well aloud. Sentences stumble, rhythms are monotonous, and word choices create unintentional cacophony or blandness. Point of view is inconsistent -- the narrator knows things the POV character cannot know, or slips between characters' thoughts without signal. The reader feels disoriented about whose consciousness they inhabit.
> A third-person limited scene where the narrator suddenly reveals another character's internal thoughts: "He watched her cross the room, wondering if she would turn back. She was thinking about whether to tell him the truth." -- The POV violation breaks trust. Le Guin warns in *Steering the Craft*: "If I am inside a character's consciousness and suddenly get yanked to another viewpoint, I feel as if I'd been pushed off a cliff."

**2 (Developing):** Prose reads reasonably well aloud but lacks musicality. Sentence rhythms are functional rather than expressive. POV is mostly consistent but occasionally wavers -- a stray thought from the wrong character, an observation the POV character could not make. The writer maintains the voice contract most of the time but does not exploit it.
> Prose that stays correctly in one character's POV but does not take advantage of it. The character perceives the world generically rather than through their particular lens. A surgeon and a poet both describe a room the same way.

**3 (Competent):** Prose reads smoothly aloud and POV is consistent. The writer avoids stumble-points and maintains the voice contract. Some passages show rhythmic awareness -- a short sentence for emphasis, a longer one for flow. But the musicality is intermittent rather than sustained, and the POV, while correct, does not yet fully color the prose. The character's way of seeing shapes some observations but not all of them.
> A scene that reads cleanly aloud with no stumbles and stays firmly in one character's POV. Some sentences have expressive rhythm. But the prose does not yet sound like this particular character thinking -- it sounds like competent narration from inside the right head. The music is present but quiet.

**4 (Strong):** Prose has rhythm and reads well aloud. Sentence length variation creates music. Stumble-points are rare. POV is consistent and exploited -- the world is filtered through the character's particular way of perceiving. The reader feels grounded in one consciousness and trusts the narrative contract. Stress patterns and word choices serve the emotional register of each passage.
> King in *On Writing*: "Good writing is often about letting go of fear and affectation. Affectation itself, beginning with the need to define some combative style, is fearful behavior." Strong prose has a natural rhythm that reflects the writer's authentic voice, filtered through the character's consciousness.

**5 (Masterful):** Prose achieves musical quality -- rhythm, sound, and cadence are as carefully composed as the content. Reading aloud reveals intentional patterns of stress, pause, and flow. POV is not merely consistent but is the organizing principle of the prose -- every word choice, every metaphor, every observation is filtered through the character's specific consciousness, education, emotional state, and sensory disposition. The character's voice and the prose's music are inseparable.
> *Their Eyes Were Watching God* by Zora Neale Hurston: "Ships at a distance have every man's wish on board. For some they come in with the tide. For others they sail forever on the horizon." The rhythm, the imagery, the oral-tradition cadence -- the prose sounds like its world. Similarly, Nabokov's *Lolita* -- Humbert Humbert's ornate, self-conscious prose is both beautiful and damning, the sound inseparable from the characterization.

### Permission and Emotional Honesty

**1 (Absent):** The prose avoids emotional risk. Scenes that demand vulnerability default to cliche, abstraction, or ironic distance. The writer flinches from difficult material -- death is handled in generalities, grief is rendered as platitude, desire is vague or euphemistic. The prose feels safe, and safety in prose reads as falseness. The reader does not believe the emotional life of the characters because the writer has not committed to it.
> A death scene where the narrator retreats into abstraction: "She felt a profound sense of loss that words could not express." The sentence announces emotion without producing it. Lamott in *Bird by Bird*: "You have to be willing to go naked. You have to write the truest sentence you know."

**2 (Developing):** The writer sometimes achieves emotional honesty but retreats in other moments. Difficult scenes are attempted but undercut by hedging, ironic distance, or sentimentality (which is the opposite of honesty -- it is a performance of feeling rather than the thing itself). The writer can access the emotion but cannot sustain it or trust it.
> A scene where a character confronts grief honestly for three paragraphs and then deflects with a joke or a cliched observation. The writer can access the emotion but cannot sustain it.

**3 (Competent):** The writer commits to emotional honesty in most scenes. Difficult material is addressed rather than avoided, and the prose generally earns its emotional moments through specificity rather than abstraction. But there are moments where the writer pulls a punch -- reaching for a generality when a specific detail would cut deeper, or softening an uncomfortable truth with a cushioning phrase. The emotional range is honest but not yet fearless.
> A grief scene where the writer renders specific details -- the unwashed coffee mug, the voicemail that has not been deleted -- but then closes with a sentence that reaches for universal meaning rather than staying in the specific pain. The honesty is real; it just does not go all the way to the bottom.

**4 (Strong):** The prose commits to emotional truth. Difficult scenes are rendered with specificity and vulnerability. The writer does not retreat from pain, desire, shame, or joy. Sentimentality is rare. The prose earns its emotional moments through concrete detail and honest observation rather than reaching for effect. The reader trusts the emotional world of the story.
> King in *On Writing*: "Good writing is often about letting go of fear and affectation." A strong passage renders grief not as "profound loss" but as the specific, irrational detail -- the dead person's shoes still by the door, the half-finished crossword puzzle on the table.

**5 (Masterful):** The prose achieves total emotional commitment. The writer holds nothing back. Difficult material is rendered with unflinching specificity that makes the reader feel it physically. There is no ironic distance where sincerity is needed, and no sentimentality where restraint is needed. The writer has given themselves full permission -- the prose goes where it must go, however uncomfortable.
> Morrison's *Beloved* -- the Middle Passage passages, Sethe's memory of the schoolteacher's nephews, the infanticide. Morrison does not flinch. The prose enters the most painful material in American history and renders it with a specificity that is almost unbearable. Similarly, Joan Didion's *The Year of Magical Thinking*: "Life changes fast. Life changes in the instant. You sit down to dinner and life as you know it ends." The simplicity is not avoidance -- it is the sound of someone who has gone past the capacity for ornament and arrived at bare truth.

### Prose Naturalness

**1 (Absent):** The prose reads as machine-generated. Em dashes appear in nearly every paragraph. Observations are consistently framed as antithesis ("Not X. It's Y."). Parallel structures and tricolon dominate. Paragraphs are suspiciously uniform in length. Hedging qualifiers stack up. The prose is technically competent but has an uncanny-valley quality -- it sounds like something, not someone.
> Prose where every other sentence contains an em dash, every observation uses contrasting-pair framing, and paragraphs march in lockstep lengths. The reader may not be able to name the problem, but they feel it: something is off about this voice.

**2 (Developing):** The prose is mostly natural but slips into artificial patterns in patches. Em dashes appear more than occasionally. Antithesis framing or parallelism surfaces in clusters, especially during exposition or thematic passages. The writer's natural voice is present but intermittently overridden by compositional habits that feel formulaic rather than organic.
> A scene where the dialogue and action feel human, but the interiority passages drift into rhetorical templates -- hedging, summarizing, or reaching for balanced contrasts that the character would not naturally produce.

**3 (Competent):** The prose feels human most of the time. Em dashes are occasional rather than habitual. Sentence patterns generally follow thought rather than rhetoric. But certain situations -- exposition, thematic reflection, passages of heightened emotion -- trigger compositional habits that read as slightly artificial. The writer's natural voice is the default, but it is not yet the only voice on the page.
> A scene where the dialogue is natural, the action is grounded, and most of the interiority feels authentic, but one paragraph of reflection falls into a pattern of balanced contrasts or rhetorical escalation that sounds more like an essay than a character thinking. The lapse is minor but noticeable to an attentive reader.

**4 (Strong):** The prose feels consistently human. Em dashes are rare and intentional. Sentence patterns follow thought rather than rhetoric. Paragraph lengths vary organically. Contractions appear naturally in interiority and dialogue. Scenes begin in the middle of something and end without summary. The reader hears a specific voice, not a craft exercise.
> King in *On Writing*: "I am convinced that fear is at the root of most bad writing." Strong natural prose has the quality of a person thinking out loud -- it follows the mind's rhythm, not a template's structure.

**5 (Masterful):** The prose is so natural that the reader forgets it is written. Voice is particular, idiosyncratic, alive. Sentence rhythms follow the character's thought patterns with such fidelity that the prose feels overheard rather than composed. Every word choice feels inevitable -- not selected from alternatives but arrived at as the only possibility.
> Denis Johnson's *Jesus' Son*: "All the great moments of profundity were over. It was just ordinary day followed by ordinary day." The prose sounds like a person remembering, not a writer composing. Similarly, Rachel Cusk's *Outline* -- the narrator's voice is so controlled and particular that every sentence feels like testimony rather than fiction.

## Character Craft

Character craft principles are scored at different scales: some per-scene, some per-act, some per-novel.

### Egri's Premise: Character Creates Conflict (Per-Scene)

**1 (Absent):** The plot happens to the character rather than emerging from who they are. The protagonist could be replaced with a different character and the story would proceed identically. Conflict arises from external coincidence rather than from the collision between a character's defining trait and the world's demands. The Egri premise -- "[trait] leads to [conflict] leads to [resolution]" -- cannot be articulated because the character's trait and the plot are disconnected.
> A protagonist who stumbles into danger by chance and escapes by chance. As Egri writes in *The Art of Dramatic Writing*: "A character who does not create conflict is not a character at all -- he is a puppet on a string, moved by the author's hand."

**2 (Developing):** The character's traits contribute to some conflicts but not others. Key plot events feel character-driven, but secondary events rely on coincidence or external force. The Egri premise can be partially articulated but breaks down at certain plot points. The writer senses that character should drive conflict but cannot yet make it happen consistently.
> A character whose stubbornness drives the central conflict but who is also swept into a subplot by coincidence. The main thread is character-driven; the subplots are not.

**3 (Competent):** The main conflict is clearly driven by the character's nature, and the Egri premise can be articulated for the central arc. But secondary conflicts and scene-level tensions do not always emerge from character -- some arise from situational convenience or plot necessity. The character drives the story's engine; they do not yet drive every gear.
> A protagonist whose central flaw creates the novel's main crisis, but whose scene-to-scene conflicts are a mix of character-driven and situational. The reader feels the character's nature shaping the big picture but not yet permeating every scene.

**4 (Strong):** The plot is clearly driven by the character's nature. The protagonist's defining traits -- virtues and flaws alike -- create the conflicts they face. The Egri premise can be cleanly articulated. Removing this specific character and replacing them with another would fundamentally alter the story. Most plot events feel like inevitable consequences of character.
> Elizabeth Bennet in *Pride and Prejudice* -- her intelligence and prejudice create every significant conflict. Her refusal of Darcy, her trust in Wickham, her eventual self-recognition -- all emerge from who she is. The premise: "Prejudice leads to misjudgment which leads to humbled self-knowledge."

**5 (Masterful):** Character and plot are so unified that separating them is impossible. Every significant event is the inevitable consequence of who the character is colliding with what the world demands. The character's greatest strength is also the source of their greatest crisis. The Egri premise operates at every level -- each scene's conflict arises from character, not situation.
> Walter White in *Breaking Bad* (novelistic in construction) -- his pride generates every escalation. Raskolnikov in *Crime and Punishment* -- his intellectual arrogance is both the cause of the murder and the engine of his unraveling. Ahab in *Moby-Dick* -- his monomaniacal obsession does not merely create the plot; it is the plot.

### Testing Characters: Questions That Expose Thin Characterization (Per-Scene)

**1 (Absent):** Characters behave generically. They react as "a person" would rather than as this specific person would. When tested by the diagnostic questions (what do they do alone? what would they never say? what do they lie about?), the answers are either unknown or generic. Characters are defined by their role in the plot rather than by their inner life.
> A protagonist described as "brave and kind" who demonstrates these traits only when the plot requires it. Between plot demands, they have no visible inner life, no idiosyncrasies, no contradictions. They are a function, not a person.

**2 (Developing):** Characters have some individuality -- particular habits, fears, or desires -- but these traits surface inconsistently. Some diagnostic questions can be answered; others cannot. Characters have an inner life that is occasionally visible but not always integrated into their behavior. They feel like sketches rather than realized people.
> A character who has a distinctive verbal tic and a clear backstory but whose emotional responses to crises are generic rather than particular. The surface is specific; the depth is not.

**3 (Competent):** Characters pass several diagnostic tests. They have specific habits, fears, and desires that are visible in scene. The reader has a sense of who these people are beyond their plot function. But some diagnostic questions still yield vague or generic answers -- the character's specificity extends to certain dimensions but not all of them. They feel like people the writer has met but not yet known intimately.
> A character whose behavior alone in a room is specific and revealing, and whose speech patterns are distinctive, but whose lies and avoidances are generic. The writer has done significant character work but has not yet reached the deepest layers.

**4 (Strong):** Characters pass most diagnostic tests. They behave distinctively when alone, when afraid, when lying. Their specific wounds, desires, and contradictions are visible in scene. They feel like people the writer knows intimately. Other characters respond to them specifically -- not as plot functions but as individuals with particular effects on those around them.
> Olive Kitteridge in Strout's novel -- every diagnostic question has a specific, surprising answer. What does she do alone? She grades math papers and eats the candy she confiscates from students. What would she never say? "I need you." Her specificity is total.

**5 (Masterful):** Characters are so fully realized that they seem to exceed the text. The reader feels the character has a life beyond the page. Every diagnostic question yields a specific, non-obvious answer that is consistent with the character's whole nature. Characters surprise the reader while remaining true to themselves -- the highest test of characterization.
> Leopold Bloom in *Ulysses* -- Joyce answers every possible diagnostic question across a single day. Bloom is the most fully realized character in English literature because Joyce withheld nothing. What does Bloom think about during a funeral? Decomposition rates, his father's suicide, whether the gravediggers get free burial plots. The specificity is inexhaustible.

### Character Web (Per-Act)

**1 (Absent):** Characters exist in isolation from one another. Relationships are defined by plot function (ally, enemy, love interest) rather than by dynamic interpersonal tension. Characters behave the same way regardless of who they are with. The web of relationships does not produce subtext, surprise, or complexity.
> Characters who relate to each other only through the plot: the hero needs the mentor's wisdom, so they interact; the hero needs the love interest's support, so they interact. Remove the plot and the relationships have no independent reason to exist.

**2 (Developing):** Some relationships have genuine dynamic tension, but others remain functional. A protagonist may have a complex relationship with one character while relating to others as plot devices. Characters sometimes change behavior depending on who they are with, but not consistently. The writer is building a web but it has more weak threads than strong ones.
> A novel with a vivid central relationship (two siblings with competing desires) but flat secondary relationships. The protagonist's interactions with their best friend, employer, and neighbor all have the same emotional register.

**3 (Competent):** Major relationships have genuine tension and specificity. Characters behave somewhat differently with different people. The web has several strong threads and the reader can feel the relationships as real rather than functional. But secondary relationships remain underdeveloped, and the web does not yet produce the emergent effects -- subtext, surprise, mutual illumination -- that a fully realized character system creates.
> A novel where the protagonist's two most important relationships are vivid and specific, a third is functional, and the rest are thin. The reader believes in the central relationships but does not feel the whole cast as an interconnected system. Removing a secondary character would barely change the dynamics.

**4 (Strong):** Major characters are defined in relation to each other. Characters behave differently with different people, revealing different facets of themselves. Relationships produce subtext -- what characters do not say to each other is as important as what they do say. The web has enough complexity that removing any major character would fundamentally alter the dynamics.
> Truby in *The Anatomy of Story*: "In great storytelling, every character exists not in isolation but in an intricate web of relationships." The web in *The Great Gatsby* exemplifies this: Nick-Gatsby, Nick-Tom, Gatsby-Daisy, Tom-Daisy, Nick-Jordan -- each relationship reveals different facets of every character.

**5 (Masterful):** The character web is a fully realized system where every relationship illuminates every character. Characters bring out specific qualities in each other that neither possesses alone. The web produces emergent effects -- the reader understands each character better by seeing how they interact with every other character. Power dynamics, affection, resentment, dependency, and admiration flow through the web in complex, realistic patterns.
> *Middlemarch* -- Eliot's web of relationships across an entire town creates a system so complex that characters who never meet still affect each other through the web. Dorothea-Casaubon-Ladislaw, Lydgate-Rosamond, Fred-Mary-the Garths -- each relationship is a complete world that connects to every other. Similarly, *The Wire* (novelistic television) -- every character is defined by their position in multiple webs (institutional, personal, economic) simultaneously.

### Character as Expression of Theme (Per-Act)

**1 (Absent):** Characters do not engage with the story's thematic questions. The theme, if present, is stated through narration or dialogue rather than embodied in the characters' conflicting worldviews. Characters are not in thematic conversation with each other. The story has a "message" that is delivered rather than explored.
> A novel about justice where only the protagonist cares about justice. The antagonist is simply "evil" rather than representing an alternative philosophy that genuinely challenges the protagonist's. The theme is a lecture, not a debate.

**2 (Developing):** Some characters embody thematic positions, but the alignment is uneven. The protagonist and antagonist may represent opposing views, but supporting characters are thematically inert. Alternatively, characters are too obviously "positioned" as mouthpieces for ideas -- the reader can see the thematic architecture rather than experiencing it. The writer knows theme should live in character but cannot yet hide the scaffolding.
> A novel where the protagonist believes in mercy and the antagonist believes in retribution, but this dynamic is so schematic that it feels like allegory. The characters serve the theme rather than living it.

**3 (Competent):** Major characters each occupy a thematic position, and the positions are distinct enough to create genuine tension. The characters feel like people first and thematic expressions second -- most of the time. But the thematic architecture occasionally shows through: a character delivers a speech that sounds more like a position paper than something they would actually say, or the collision between worldviews is too neatly arranged. The theme is alive in the characters but has not yet become invisible.
> A novel where three characters represent different responses to the central question, and their conflicts genuinely explore the theme. But one key scene feels like a debate rather than a drama -- the characters articulate their positions too cleanly, and the reader can see the writer arranging the argument rather than letting it emerge.

**4 (Strong):** Major characters each embody a distinct answer to the story's central question, and these answers collide through the plot. The theme emerges from the collision rather than being imposed. Characters feel like people first and thematic expressions second. The reader may not be able to articulate the theme but feels its presence in the tensions between characters.
> *Crime and Punishment* -- Raskolnikov, Sonya, Svidrigailov, and Porfiry each embody a different response to the question of whether extraordinary people are bound by ordinary morality. The theme is a living argument, not a thesis statement.

**5 (Masterful):** Every character is a fully realized human being who also, by their very nature, embodies a position in the story's thematic debate. The theme is not "about" something external -- it emerges inevitably from who these particular characters are and what happens when they collide. The thematic conversation is rich enough that the novel does not resolve into a single message but remains productively ambiguous.
> *The Brothers Karamazov* by Dostoevsky -- Dmitri (passion), Ivan (intellect), Alyosha (faith), and Fyodor (appetite) each embody a different relationship to God, morality, and freedom. The novel's theme is the totality of their interactions, not reducible to any single character's position. Similarly, *Beloved* -- Sethe, Paul D, Denver, and Beloved embody different relationships to the past, to freedom, and to memory, and the novel's meaning lives in the space between their perspectives.

---

### Want/Need (Per-Novel)

**1 (Absent):** The character's want is unclear or changes arbitrarily. There is no identifiable need (the unconscious thing the character must learn or become). The gap between want and need -- the engine of character arc -- does not exist because one or both poles are undefined. The character may have a goal but not a psychologically coherent desire structure.
> A protagonist who "wants to save the world" but has no personal stake, no internal contradiction, and no unconscious need that the journey forces them to confront. The want is a plot objective, not a character drive.

**2 (Developing):** Want is clear. Need is present but underdeveloped or stated too explicitly (a character literally says "maybe I need to learn to trust people"). The gap between want and need exists but does not drive the plot consistently. The writer has established both poles but has not yet made the tension between them the engine of the story.
> A character who wants to win a competition and needs to learn that winning is not everything. The arc is real but predictable, and the need surfaces through dialogue rather than through the pressure of events.

**3 (Competent):** Want and need are both defined and in tension. The gap between them is visible and drives the main arc. The character pursues their want while the narrative creates pressure toward their need. But the tension is not yet the story's primary engine -- it coexists with plot mechanics that operate independently of the want/need dynamic. The arc is present and functional but does not permeate every scene.
> A character whose want and need are clear and whose climactic moment involves choosing between them. But in the middle of the novel, several scenes advance the plot without engaging the want/need tension. The arc has a strong beginning and end but sags in the middle where the framework should be exerting constant pressure.

**4 (Strong):** Want and need are clearly defined and in productive tension. The character pursues their want throughout the story while the narrative forces a confrontation with their need. The gap between the two is the source of the story's central tension. The character's growth (or tragic refusal to grow) feels earned by the events of the plot.
> Elizabeth Bennet -- wants to protect her independence and judgment; needs to recognize that her judgment is compromised by prejudice. The entire plot of *Pride and Prejudice* exists in the gap between these two. The climactic moment (her reading Darcy's letter) is the moment want and need collide.

**5 (Masterful):** The want/need structure is psychologically complex -- the want may shift, the need may be layered, and the relationship between them may be paradoxical. The character's want and need illuminate universal human tensions. The moment of choice between want and need is the story's climax, and it feels both surprising and inevitable. Whether the character achieves both, sacrifices one for the other, or fails at both, the resolution illuminates the human condition.
> Jay Gatsby -- wants to recapture the past (Daisy); needs to recognize that the past cannot be recaptured and that his idealized vision has consumed his actual life. The want and need are not merely in tension; they are structurally impossible to reconcile, which is why the story is a tragedy. The want/need framework is so deeply integrated that it shapes every scene, every image, every line of the novel.

### Wound/Lie (Per-Novel)

**1 (Absent):** The character has no discernible backstory wound, or the wound is trivial and does not shape behavior. There is no "lie" -- no false belief that the character has constructed to protect themselves from the wound's truth. The character enters the story as a blank slate or with a wound that is mentioned but never shown to affect their choices.
> A protagonist whose parents divorced, which is mentioned once in a flashback but never influences any decision they make. The wound is decorative, not structural.

**2 (Developing):** A wound is present and a lie is identifiable, but the connection between them is thin or over-explained. The character's backstory is told rather than shown shaping present behavior. The lie may be stated explicitly rather than demonstrated through choices. The writer has the framework but has not yet made it live in the character's behavior.
> A character who was betrayed by a friend and now "has trust issues." The lie (people will always betray you) is stated in dialogue rather than revealed through a pattern of behavior that the reader comes to understand.

**3 (Competent):** The wound is specific and the lie it generated is identifiable through the character's behavior rather than through exposition. The reader can see the wound-lie dynamic shaping some of the character's choices. But the dynamic does not yet permeate the character's entire way of being -- it surfaces in key moments and recedes in others. The wound feels like an important piece of backstory rather than the psychic foundation of the character.
> A character whose wound and lie are visible in their romantic relationships but not in their professional life or friendships. The wound shapes certain choices convincingly but does not yet color everything. The reader understands the dynamic intellectually but does not feel it as the character's operating system.

**4 (Strong):** The wound is specific and psychologically convincing. The lie it generated shapes the character's present-day behavior in ways the character does not fully understand. The reader can identify the wound-lie dynamic through the character's actions, avoidances, and reactions before it is ever made explicit. The confrontation with truth feels like a genuine crisis.
> Kathy in *Never Let Me Go* -- the wound (being created for a purpose she cannot escape) generates the lie (if she follows the rules, she will be treated as fully human). Every choice she makes is shaped by this lie, and the reader sees it before she does.

**5 (Masterful):** The wound is not merely backstory but the psychic foundation of the character. The lie permeates every layer of the character's behavior -- their voice, their relationships, their choices, even their way of perceiving the world. The confrontation with truth does not resolve neatly; it cracks the character open and the reader sees all the layers at once. The wound/lie dynamic feels not like a craft framework applied to a character but like the lived experience of a real human being.
> Sethe in *Beloved* -- the wound (slavery, the schoolteacher, the Crossing) generates multiple lies that she cannot separate from survival itself. The confrontation with truth (Beloved's return, Paul D's challenge) does not heal the wound; it reopens it so that genuine healing might eventually begin. The wound is the novel. Similarly, Stevens in *The Remains of the Day* -- the wound (his father's servility and death) generates the lie (dignity requires suppressing all personal feeling). The entire novel is the lie slowly, painfully cracking.

### Flaws as Strengths, Strengths as Flaws (Per-Novel)

**1 (Absent):** Character flaws are bolted on -- "she's brave but also reckless" -- without any sense that the flaw and the strength are the same quality under different pressures. Strengths and flaws exist on separate lists rather than being facets of a unified personality. The character feels assembled from traits rather than grown from a coherent psychology.
> A character described as "loyal but too trusting." These are presented as separate attributes rather than as one quality (deep commitment to others) that serves them in some situations and exposes them in others.

**2 (Developing):** The writer understands that strengths and flaws are related and attempts to link them, but the connection is superficial or inconsistent. A strength becomes a flaw in one scene but reverts to pure strength in the next. The writer has the concept but has not yet made it function as a coherent dynamic across the story.
> A character whose determination is shown as a flaw in one scene (refusing to retreat from a bad plan) but as a pure virtue in the next (persevering through hardship) without any sense that the character's relationship to this quality has evolved.

**3 (Competent):** The link between strength and flaw is visible and functions in the story's key moments. The reader can see that the character's defining quality cuts both ways. But the dynamic is not yet fully explored -- the narrative demonstrates the duality in a few important scenes without systematically testing the boundary between help and harm. The concept is realized but not exhausted.
> A character whose intelligence is shown as both asset and liability in the story's central crisis, but whose other scenes use the intelligence purely as a strength. The duality is present at the climax but not woven through the fabric of the entire narrative.

**4 (Strong):** Strengths and flaws are clearly derived from the same core quality. The character's nature serves them in some contexts and betrays them in others, and the narrative demonstrates this convincingly. The reader understands that the flaw cannot be "fixed" without also destroying the strength. The character's internal tension is a coherent expression of a unified personality.
> Achilles' rage -- the quality that makes him the greatest warrior is the quality that destroys everything he loves. The flaw is the strength pushed past its limit. The story is the exploration of that limit.

**5 (Masterful):** The unity of strength and flaw is the character's defining complexity. The narrative creates situations that systematically test the boundary between when the quality helps and when it harms. The character's arc is defined by their evolving relationship with this double-edged quality. The reader feels the character's nature as a lived paradox rather than an intellectual concept.
> Emma Woodhouse in Austen's *Emma* -- her intelligence and social confidence (strengths) are inseparable from her meddling and snobbery (flaws). She cannot stop being clever, and her cleverness is both her greatest asset and the source of every significant error she makes. The novel is a precision instrument for testing the boundary between insight and interference. Similarly, Hamlet -- his capacity for reflection is both what makes him profound and what prevents him from acting. To "fix" the flaw would be to destroy the character.

### Voice as Character (Per-Novel)

**1 (Absent):** All characters sound alike. Dialogue could be reassigned between characters without the reader noticing. Characters notice the same things, use the same metaphors, and have the same verbal rhythms. The narrative voice, if first-person, is generic rather than particular. There is no sense that language reveals character.
> A novel with six characters whose dialogue is distinguishable only by dialogue tags. Remove the tags and all six speak with the same vocabulary, sentence structure, and observational habits.

**2 (Developing):** Some characters have distinctive verbal tics (an accent, a catchphrase, a tendency toward long sentences), but these are surface-level markers rather than deep expressions of character. The main character has a somewhat distinctive voice, but secondary characters blend together. The writer is using voice as costume rather than identity.
> A character given a Southern dialect whose actual perceptions and thought patterns are identical to every other character's. The voice is a costume, not an identity.

**3 (Competent):** Major characters have distinct voices that go beyond surface tics. The reader can usually identify speakers from their dialogue without tags. Characters' perceptions show some differentiation -- they notice different things or reach for different kinds of comparisons. But the differentiation is not yet deep enough to feel like fully distinct consciousnesses. The voices are distinguishable but not yet irreplaceable.
> A novel where the two main characters speak differently and notice different things about a room, but their internal rhythms and emotional registers are more similar than distinct. The reader can tell them apart but does not yet feel that each character's voice IS their character.

**4 (Strong):** Major characters are vocally distinct in ways that go beyond surface markers. They notice different things, use different metaphors, and leave different things unsaid. Dialogue reveals education, emotional state, and relationship to the listener. The reader can identify the speaker from the content and cadence of the speech without a dialogue tag. Narrative voice, if first-person, is particular and consistent.
> Each character in *A Visit from the Goon Squad* has a distinctive way of seeing and speaking that reflects their particular psychology, era, and circumstances. Sasha's narration is different from Bennie's, which is different from Lou's -- not just in what they talk about but in how they perceive.

**5 (Masterful):** Voice IS character. The way a character speaks and perceives is so deeply integrated with who they are that separating voice from character is impossible. Every word choice, every silence, every metaphor reveals the character's inner world. First-person narrators have voices so distinctive that the voice itself is a primary source of meaning. Dialogue creates entire characters in a few lines.
> Humbert Humbert in *Lolita* -- the ornate, self-justifying, endlessly qualifying prose is not description of character; it is the character. The voice reveals everything: Humbert's intelligence, his narcissism, his desperate need to aestheticize his crime, his genuine and corrupted capacity for beauty. Similarly, Huck Finn's voice in Twain's novel -- the vernacular grammar, the moral confusion, the innocent eye -- creates a character that could not exist without this specific voice.

## Rules to Break

These seven rules are scored per-scene. The scoring is inverted from typical rubrics: a score of 5 means the rule was either followed masterfully or broken intentionally with clear artistic purpose. A score of 1 means the rule was violated in ways that undermine the prose.

### Show, Don't Tell

**1 (Absent):** Emotional turning points are consistently told rather than shown, and the telling is not a deliberate narrative choice. The reader is informed about characters' feelings through labels ("he was angry," "she felt betrayed") rather than through action, dialogue, and sensory detail that would let the reader experience those feelings directly. When telling occurs, it adds no value that showing could not have provided more powerfully.
> "John was devastated by the loss. He had never felt so alone in his life. The grief was overwhelming." -- Three sentences of emotional labeling that produce no feeling in the reader. Contrast with: "He kept setting the table for two."

**2 (Developing):** The writer shows important moments some of the time but defaults to telling under pressure -- particularly in scenes of high emotion, where the instinct to explain overrides the instinct to dramatize. When the rule is broken, it is not broken for effect but because the writer has not yet found their way into the scene.
> A scene that shows a character's tension through physical detail (drumming fingers, checking the clock) for two paragraphs, then tells: "She couldn't take the waiting anymore. It was making her crazy." The telling undercuts what the showing already accomplished.

**3 (Competent):** The writer shows pivotal moments more often than not and tells primarily for transitions and compression. The instinct to dramatize important moments is present and mostly reliable. But the writer occasionally tells at moments that would benefit from showing, or adds a telling sentence to a scene that was already doing its work through showing. The balance is functional but not yet precise.
> A scene where the confrontation is fully dramatized and effective, but the writer adds a closing sentence: "It was the worst conversation of her life." The scene already communicated this; the telling sentence is unnecessary but not devastating. The writer trusts the showing most of the time but not quite all of the time.

**4 (Strong):** The rule is followed consistently where it matters. Pivotal emotional moments are shown through action, dialogue, and concrete detail. When telling occurs, it serves a clear purpose: efficient transition, necessary compression, or a narrative voice that earns the authority to tell. The writer distinguishes between moments that demand dramatization and moments that benefit from summary.
> Austen's narration in *Pride and Prejudice* tells constantly -- "It is a truth universally acknowledged..." -- but the telling voice is the point. The ironic distance IS the effect. The rule is broken with full awareness of what is gained.

**5 (Masterful):** The writer either follows the rule with such skill that emotional scenes land with full physical impact, or breaks the rule in ways that could not be improved by showing. The telling, if present, is more powerful than showing would be -- it adds a layer of narrative authority, ironic distance, or emotional compression that dramatization could not achieve.
> Dickens opening *A Tale of Two Cities*: "It was the best of times, it was the worst of times..." -- pure telling that is more powerful than any scene could be. Morrison in *Beloved*: "Freeing yourself was one thing; claiming ownership of that freed self was another." -- A told insight that lands with the force of a shown scene because of everything that precedes it.

---

### Avoid Adverbs

**1 (Absent):** Adverbs are ubiquitous and consistently weaken the prose. Nearly every dialogue tag includes an adverb ("he said angrily," "she whispered softly"). Action verbs are routinely modified instead of replaced ("walked slowly" for "shuffled," "ate quickly" for "wolfed"). The adverbs are crutches for weak verbs rather than genuine modifications of meaning.
> "'I can't believe you did that,' she said incredulously. He walked slowly toward the door, opening it carefully. 'Get out,' she said firmly." -- Every adverb signals a verb that should have been replaced.

**2 (Developing):** Adverbs are reduced but still appear in habitual spots -- particularly dialogue tags and action sequences. Some adverbs genuinely modify meaning; others are vestigial. The writer is aware of the rule but has not fully internalized it.
> A scene where most verbs are strong and precise, but dialogue tags still carry adverbs: "said quietly," "said hesitantly." The writer has addressed adverbs in action prose but not in dialogue attribution.

**3 (Competent):** Adverbs are infrequent and most of them earn their place. The writer defaults to strong verbs and uses adverbs only when they add information the verb does not carry. But occasional adverbs appear out of habit rather than purpose -- a "said softly" where the context already establishes volume, a "walked quickly" where "hurried" would serve better. The discipline is present but not yet complete.
> Prose where adverbs appear perhaps once per page, and most are defensible, but two or three per chapter are vestigial. The writer's verb choices are generally strong enough to carry the load, with occasional lapses back into the adverb-as-crutch habit.

**4 (Strong):** Adverbs are rare and purposeful. Verbs carry their own weight. When an adverb appears, it modifies meaning rather than propping up a weak verb. The writer has internalized the principle that strong verbs rarely need modification and consistently chooses the precise verb.
> "She smiled coldly" -- the adverb works because it contradicts the verb, creating meaning that neither word could produce alone. This is a deliberate use, not a crutch.

**5 (Masterful):** Adverbs either are virtually absent (the writer has fully replaced the habit with precise verb selection) or appear only when they create effects no other construction could achieve. The prose's energy comes from verbs and nouns. When the rule is broken, the adverb is startling and precise -- it earns its place by doing something no other word could do in that position.
> Hemingway's prose demonstrates near-total adverb elimination, with force generated entirely by verb precision and noun specificity. When he does use an adverb ("and afterward, in the dark, it was as though she were holding on to nothing"), it carries enormous weight precisely because adverbs are so rare in his prose.

---

### Avoid Passive Voice

**1 (Absent):** Passive voice is the default construction. Actions happen to characters rather than being performed by them. Sentences consistently use "was [verb]ed" constructions that drain energy and obscure agency. The passive voice is not being used for effect -- it is being used by default, often because the writer has not identified the agent of the action.
> "The door was opened and the room was entered. The letter was read and tears were shed." -- Four passive constructions in two sentences. No one does anything; things just happen.

**2 (Developing):** Active voice predominates but passive constructions appear in clusters, particularly in exposition and description. The writer defaults to passive when uncertain about who is doing what. Some passive constructions serve a purpose (emphasizing the recipient of action); others are habitual.
> A scene with mostly active prose that slips into passive during a descriptive paragraph: "The garden was maintained by the elderly groundskeeper. The hedges had been trimmed and the paths were swept." The passivity matches the stillness of the setting, but it is more likely accident than intention.

**3 (Competent):** Active voice drives most of the prose. Passive constructions are occasional and most of them are defensible -- emphasizing the action over the actor, or varying sentence rhythm. But some passive constructions are habitual rather than purposeful, particularly in exposition or descriptive passages. The writer uses active voice by default but has not yet made every passive construction a deliberate choice.
> Prose where passive voice appears a few times per scene, and most instances have a plausible justification, but one or two per scene are simply the writer not noticing. "The decision was made to leave early" in a passage where the character's agency is the point -- the passive obscures what should be active.

**4 (Strong):** Active voice drives the prose. Passive voice appears only when the writer deliberately wants to emphasize the receiver over the actor, create a sense of institutional power, or obscure agency for dramatic effect. Every passive construction could be converted to active, but the writer has chosen passive for a reason.
> "The promotion was denied." -- Passive voice used intentionally to emphasize the systemic, faceless nature of the denial. The agent is absent because the absence is the point. The character is acted upon by a system, and the grammar mirrors the experience.

**5 (Masterful):** Voice (active/passive) is deployed as a precision instrument. Active voice gives scenes energy, agency, and momentum. Passive voice, when it appears, creates specific effects: helplessness, institutional power, the erasure of individual agency, mystery about who acted. The reader does not notice the grammar -- they only feel its effects.
> Kafka's prose in *The Trial* and *The Metamorphosis* uses passive and impersonal constructions to create the feeling of bureaucratic helplessness: "Someone must have been telling lies about Josef K." The opening sentence of *The Trial* uses passive voice not from weakness but to establish that Josef K. lives in a world where things are done to you by unnamed forces. The grammar IS the theme.

---

### Write What You Know

**1 (Absent):** The prose lacks sensory and emotional authenticity. Experiences outside the writer's direct knowledge are rendered in vague generalities or Hollywood cliches. Characters in unfamiliar professions, cultures, or situations behave according to stereotype rather than observed reality. Emotional experiences are abstract rather than grounded in the specific physical and psychological texture of lived reality.
> A writer with no medical experience writing a surgery scene that consists entirely of TV-drama tropes: "Scalpel. Sponge. His heart rate is dropping! We're losing him!" The scene has no texture of real surgical experience -- no smell, no specific detail, no emotional reality beyond cliche.

**2 (Developing):** The writer shows some research or emotional extrapolation but has not fully inhabited unfamiliar material. Details are present but occasionally wrong or generic. Emotional experiences are honest in some scenes but ring false in others -- particularly when the writer reaches beyond their emotional range.
> A war novel by a writer who has researched combat but has not found the specific sensory and emotional details that would make it feel lived-in. The facts are right but the feeling is secondhand.

**3 (Competent):** The writer renders unfamiliar material with reasonable authenticity. Research is evident and details are mostly right. Emotional experiences are convincing in most scenes. But the prose occasionally betrays a gap between knowledge and inhabitation -- a detail that is accurate but feels imported rather than organic, an emotional beat that is plausible but not quite lived-in. The reader mostly trusts the writer's authority but senses they are working from notes rather than from bone-deep knowledge.
> A historical novel where the period details are well-researched and the characters behave plausibly, but certain scenes feel like the writer is describing a world rather than inhabiting one. The research shows, which means it has not yet been fully transformed into fiction.

**4 (Strong):** The writer either draws on genuine experience with authority or has researched and empathized deeply enough that unfamiliar material feels authentic. Emotional truths are specific and convincing. The reader trusts the writer's authority over the material, whether that authority comes from experience, research, or imaginative empathy.
> Hilary Mantel's *Wolf Hall* -- Mantel did not live in Tudor England, but the novel's sensory and psychological detail is so specific and researched that it feels like memory. "Write what you know" becomes "write what you can know through rigorous empathy and research."

**5 (Masterful):** The material feels not merely researched or experienced but inhabited. The writer has transformed their knowledge -- whether experiential, researched, or empathetic -- into something that reads as absolute authority. The reader never questions the writer's access to the material. Emotional truths are so specific and honest that the reader recognizes them even if they have never had the same experience.
> Morrison writing about slavery in *Beloved* draws on research, oral history, and imaginative empathy so thoroughly that the novel reads as testimony. Tolstoy's battle scenes in *War and Peace* combine his military experience with imaginative extension to create scenes that soldiers have called the most accurate depiction of combat ever written. The rule is transcended: the writer knows because they have committed fully to knowing.

---

### Never Open with Weather/Dreams/Waking Up

**1 (Absent):** The scene opens with weather, a dream, or a character waking up, and the choice delays the introduction of character and conflict with no compensating benefit. The weather is generic ("It was a dark and stormy night"), the dream is a cheap misdirect or transparent symbolism, or the waking-up sequence is passive and uneventful. The opening could be cut entirely without losing anything.
> "She woke up and stretched. Sunlight streamed through the window. She got out of bed and went to the bathroom." -- Three sentences of non-event before the scene begins. Bulwer-Lytton's "It was a dark and stormy night" is the canonical example of weather-as-delay.

**2 (Developing):** The opening uses weather, a dream, or waking up, and the choice has some justification -- the weather is relevant, the dream contains information -- but the execution is not strong enough to justify breaking the convention. The opening works but a different opening would work better.
> A storm opening in a survival novel -- the weather is genuinely relevant, but the description is generic rather than specific enough to justify its position at the start of the scene.

**3 (Competent):** The rule is generally followed -- most scenes open with character in action or conflict. When the rule is broken, the break has a justification that the reader can see even if it is not fully compelling. The writer understands the convention and respects it, breaking it only when they have a reason, though that reason is not always strong enough to make the break feel inevitable.
> A novel where most scenes open in medias res, but one chapter opens with a weather description that establishes mood effectively if unremarkably. The opening works well enough -- it is not a violation that weakens the scene -- but a stronger opening was available. The writer's instinct to avoid the trap is present; their execution of exceptions is adequate rather than artful.

**4 (Strong):** The rule is either followed (the scene opens with character in action or conflict) or broken with clear purpose. If weather opens the scene, it is because the weather IS the conflict or the atmosphere IS the point and is rendered with specificity that justifies its prominence. If a dream opens the scene, the dream is not a cheap misdirect but serves a genuine narrative purpose. The reader understands why this opening was chosen.
> The opening of *Bleak House* by Dickens: "London. Michaelmas Term lately over, and the Lord Chancellor sitting in Lincoln's Inn Hall. Implacable November weather." -- Weather opens the novel, but the weather IS the novel's metaphor. The fog is the Chancery system. The rule is broken with total awareness of why it exists.

**5 (Masterful):** If the rule is broken, the breaking is itself a masterwork. The weather/dream/waking-up opening is so perfectly crafted that it could not be replaced. The opening does triple duty: it establishes atmosphere, introduces character, and creates tension simultaneously. If the rule is followed, the scene opens with such precision that the reader is immediately inside the story.
> The opening of *One Hundred Years of Solitude*: "Many years later, as he faced the firing squad, Colonel Aureliano Buendia was to remember that distant afternoon when his father took him to discover ice." -- This is technically a memory opening, which is adjacent to the dream opening, but it is so brilliantly constructed that it transcends the category. Similarly, the opening of *Rebecca* by Daphne du Maurier: "Last night I dreamt I went to Manderley again." -- A dream opening that is among the most famous first lines in English literature, because the dream is not a misdirect but the novel's emotional architecture in miniature.

### Avoid Said-Bookisms

**1 (Absent):** Dialogue tags are a thesaurus exercise. Characters exclaim, declare, opine, query, riposte, ejaculate, expostulate, and vociferate. Every tag calls attention to itself and away from the dialogue. The reader notices the attribution rather than the speech. The writer appears to believe that "said" is boring and must be avoided.
> "'I can't believe it!' she exclaimed. 'Neither can I,' he retorted. 'What shall we do?' she queried. 'I know exactly what,' he declared." -- Four said-bookisms in four lines. The tags are louder than the dialogue.

**2 (Developing):** The writer mostly uses "said" but occasionally reaches for alternatives without clear purpose. "Asked," "replied," and "whispered" appear appropriately; "exclaimed," "declared," and "intoned" appear when "said" would serve better. The writer has learned the rule but still feels "said" is repetitive.
> Dialogue that is cleanly tagged with "said" for three exchanges and then, as if embarrassed by the repetition, switches to "noted" or "observed." The variation serves the writer's anxiety, not the reader's experience.

**3 (Competent):** "Said" is the default tag and is mostly invisible. The writer uses "asked" and "whispered" appropriately and rarely reaches for more exotic alternatives. Occasionally an unnecessary tag appears -- a "replied" or "responded" where "said" would do, or a tag on a line of dialogue where the speaker is already clear from context. The writer understands the principle and follows it with minor lapses.
> Dialogue where "said" handles most of the work, action beats identify some speakers, and the occasional "murmured" or "called" appears with justification. One or two tags per scene are unnecessary but do not distract. The writer is close to invisible attribution but has not yet achieved it completely.

**4 (Strong):** "Said" is the default and is genuinely invisible. Alternatives appear only when they carry information that "said" cannot: "whispered" (volume), "shouted" (volume), "lied" (dramatic irony). Many dialogue exchanges drop tags entirely, using action beats or context to identify the speaker. The reader never notices attribution.
> "'I'll go.' She picked up her coat. 'Don't.' 'I have to.' He didn't follow her to the door." -- No "said" at all. Speaker identification through action beats and the rhythm of the exchange. Clean, invisible, effective.

**5 (Masterful):** Dialogue attribution is a precision tool. Tags disappear when the dialogue carries itself. When a tag appears, it is either invisible ("said") or deliberately chosen to add a specific layer of meaning. The writer may use said-bookisms -- "lied," "conceded," "admitted" -- but only when the tag creates an effect that nothing else could: dramatic irony, a shift in power dynamics, an emotional revelation. The reader is completely inside the conversation.
> Hemingway's "Hills Like White Elephants" -- nearly all dialogue is untagged. The reader tracks the speakers through rhythm and content. When "said" appears, its rarity gives it weight. McCarthy's *Blood Meridian* removes quotation marks entirely, integrating speech into narration so that the question of attribution becomes part of the novel's style. Elmore Leonard's dialogue achieves maximum naturalism through minimal attribution and precisely calibrated action beats.

### Kill Your Darlings

**1 (Absent):** The prose retains passages that serve the writer's ego rather than the story. Beautiful sentences that have no narrative purpose. Clever metaphors that call attention to the author's skill rather than the character's experience. Beloved scenes that bring the plot to a halt. The writer's attachment to their best writing is visible, and that attachment is harming the work.
> A three-page lyrical passage about the landscape that stops the narrative dead. The writing may be genuinely beautiful, but it does not serve character, advance plot, or deepen theme. It exists because the writer was proud of it. As Faulkner (apocryphally) said: "In writing, you must kill all your darlings."

**2 (Developing):** Most self-indulgent passages have been pruned, but a few remain -- usually the writer's favorite scenes or turns of phrase. The writer has learned to cut but still protects their most treasured material, even when that material is not serving the story. The writer can kill minor darlings but flinches from the major ones.
> A novel that moves with good pace except for one scene that clearly exists because the writer loved writing it. The scene may contain the book's best prose, but it does not belong where it is -- or at all.

**3 (Competent):** The writer demonstrates willingness to cut and has removed most passages that do not serve the story. Pacing rarely stalls for self-indulgent prose. But a careful reader can still identify moments where the writer's attachment to a phrase, image, or passage has slightly overridden their editorial judgment. The darlings that remain are small and defensible -- they do not damage the work, but they do not quite earn their place either.
> A novel with strong pacing and purposeful prose that contains a few passages -- a description that lingers a beat too long, a metaphor that is beautiful but slightly tangential -- where the writer's pleasure in the writing is faintly visible. These moments do not harm the reader's experience, but a ruthless editor would trim them.

**4 (Strong):** The writer demonstrates willingness to cut anything that does not serve the story. Beautiful writing exists throughout, but it consistently serves character, plot, or theme. When a passage is lyrical, it is lyrical for a reason. The writer can distinguish between "good writing" and "writing that is good for this story" and consistently chooses the latter.
> A novel where every memorable passage also advances the narrative. No scene exists solely to showcase the writer's abilities. The prose is consistently excellent, which means it is consistently purposeful.

**5 (Masterful):** Every passage, no matter how beautiful, serves the story. The writer's darlings have either been killed or have been so thoroughly integrated into the narrative's purpose that they are no longer darlings -- they are just good writing doing its job. Alternatively, the writer breaks the rule by retaining a passage of such brilliance that its presence elevates the work even if it does not strictly "serve" the plot -- the darling is kept because it IS the story's purpose.
> Morrison's prose in *Beloved* is full of passages that could be called "darlings" -- lyrical, poetic, extraordinary. But every one of them serves the novel's emotional and thematic architecture. "She is a friend of my mind. She gather me, man. The pieces I am, she gather them and give them back to me in all the right order." -- Beautiful writing that is also the most precise articulation of the character's need. The darling and the purpose are inseparable.

## Narrative Frameworks

These seven frameworks are scored per-act. The score reflects how effectively the chosen framework's principles are realized in the act's structure and pacing.

### Campbell's Monomyth / Hero's Journey

**1 (Absent):** The framework is either absent or applied as a rigid checklist. Key beats (Call to Adventure, Ordeal, Return) are present but feel mechanical -- they happen because the template demands them, not because the story has earned them. The hero's transformation is declared rather than demonstrated. The framework constrains rather than illuminates.
> A fantasy novel that hits every monomyth beat on schedule but without emotional weight. The hero crosses the threshold because it is page 50, not because the story's logic demands it. Campbell himself warned in *The Hero with a Thousand Faces*: "The latest incarnation of Oedipus, the continued romance of Beauty and the Beast, stand this afternoon on the corner of Forty-second Street and Fifth Avenue, waiting for the traffic light to change" -- the archetype must live in the specific.

**2 (Developing):** Major beats are present and mostly earned, but transitions between them feel rushed or unearned. The hero's internal transformation does not keep pace with the external plot milestones. The writer understands the framework but cannot yet make every beat feel organic to this particular story.
> A hero who Refuses the Call in a scene that works dramatically but then Crosses the Threshold in the next scene without sufficient pressure to justify the change. The beats are present but the connective tissue between them is weak.

**3 (Competent):** The monomyth beats are present and most are earned by the story's internal logic. The hero's journey has emotional weight at its key moments. But some beats feel more template-driven than story-driven -- a reader familiar with Campbell might notice the framework behind the curtain. The structure works but has not yet been fully absorbed into the story's own texture.
> A hero's journey where the Call, Threshold, and Ordeal all land with dramatic force, but the Mentor figure, the Tests/Allies/Enemies, and the Resurrection feel more like obligations than organic story moments. The framework is mostly serving the story, but the story is also serving the framework in places.

**4 (Strong):** The monomyth beats are realized with emotional and dramatic weight. The hero's external journey mirrors an internal transformation. Key beats feel inevitable rather than imposed. The framework has been absorbed into the story's own logic -- a reader unfamiliar with Campbell would not recognize a template but would feel the story's satisfying shape.
> *The Lord of the Rings* (particularly Frodo's arc) -- Campbell's beats are present but so deeply integrated into the story's own mythology that they feel discovered rather than applied. The Ordeal, the Abyss, the Return -- each is earned by hundreds of pages of accumulated story.

**5 (Masterful):** The monomyth is either so deeply integrated that it becomes invisible (the archetypal pattern felt but never seen) or is deliberately subverted in ways that create new meaning. The hero's journey illuminates the particular story without constraining it. The framework's limitations (masculine bias, single-hero focus) are acknowledged or transcended.
> *Star Wars* (original trilogy) -- Lucas studied Campbell explicitly, and the result is a monomyth realization so clean that it became the modern reference point for the framework. More nuanced: *The Left Hand of Darkness* by Le Guin uses the journey structure but subverts its gender assumptions, creating a monomyth that questions the mono.

### Three-Act Structure

**1 (Absent):** The act has no discernible internal structure. There is no sense of escalating commitment. The inciting incident (Act I), midpoint shift (Act II), or climax (Act III) is missing, muted, or misplaced. Pacing problems are severe: the beginning is too long, the middle sags, or the ending is rushed. The act feels like a container rather than a pressure curve.
> An Act Two that is simply "things happen" without escalation, midpoint reversal, or building commitment. The act is a sequence of events rather than a rising (or falling) curve of tension.

**2 (Developing):** The act's key structural beats are present but their placement or execution is off. An Act One that takes too long to reach the inciting incident. An Act Two whose midpoint shift is weak. An Act Three whose climax arrives without sufficient buildup. The writer understands the structure but cannot yet place its beats with precision.
> An Act One with a clear inciting incident that arrives too late -- the reader has been waiting for thirty pages for the story to begin. The beat exists; it is just in the wrong place.

**3 (Competent):** The act is structured with identifiable, reasonably placed beats. The inciting incident disrupts the status quo, the midpoint shifts direction, the climax resolves (or fails to resolve) the central tension. Pacing is generally controlled. But the beats lack the force they could have -- the inciting incident is clear but not jolting, the midpoint shift is present but not revelatory, the climax works but does not feel inevitable. The structure is correct without being powerful.
> An act where all the beats are present and placed within the right range, and the reader is carried through without confusion. But the experience is smooth rather than gripping -- the structure serves the story without electrifying it. The reader feels guided rather than pulled.

**4 (Strong):** The act is well-structured with clear, well-placed beats. The inciting incident disrupts the status quo with sufficient force. The midpoint genuinely shifts the story's direction. The climax is earned and powerful. Pacing is controlled -- the reader feels pulled through the act by escalating commitment.
> McKee's analysis in *Story* of the three-act structure as a "pressure curve" rather than three containers is realized: each act builds upon the last, with the protagonist increasingly committed to a course of action from which they cannot retreat.

**5 (Masterful):** The act's structure generates maximum narrative force while feeling organic rather than imposed. Beats land with the surprise-yet-inevitability that characterizes great storytelling. The structure serves the story's specific needs -- a quiet literary novel uses the same structural principles as a thriller but realizes them differently. The reader experiences the structure as momentum, not as architecture.
> *Jane Eyre* by Charlotte Bronte -- the three-act structure (Lowood/Thornfield/Moor House) is so clean that it teaches the form, but each act also subverts expectations. The Act Two climax (the interrupted wedding) is one of the most powerful structural turns in English literature because it is simultaneously the moment of greatest hope and greatest devastation.

### Save the Cat Beat Sheet

**1 (Absent):** Beats are either absent or so mechanically applied that the reader can feel the template. Scenes exist to satisfy the beat sheet rather than to serve the story. The "Fun and Games" section is disconnected from the theme. The "All Is Lost" moment is arbitrary. The structure produces pacing that feels artificial -- events happen on schedule rather than because the story demands them.
> A novel where each chapter corresponds to a beat, and the reader can sense the writer checking boxes. The Catalyst arrives on page 25 because the formula says so, not because the story's logic demands it at that point.

**2 (Developing):** Most beats are present and most serve the story, but the formula is still visible. Some beats feel organic; others feel forced. The writer has internalized the pacing logic but has not yet learned to make the beats feel like they belong to this specific story rather than to the template.
> A commercial novel with good pacing that occasionally feels like it is following a recipe. The "Dark Night of the Soul" is present but feels mandated rather than earned.

**3 (Competent):** The beat sheet's pacing logic is mostly absorbed into the story. Major beats serve both plot and emotional arc. The reader is carried through the story at a good clip. But a reader familiar with the framework can still spot the architecture -- the "Midpoint" is identifiable as a midpoint, the "All Is Lost" is identifiable as an all-is-lost. The structure is working well but has not yet become invisible.
> A novel where the pacing is strong and the beats land with enough force that the reader is engaged, but where a structural analysis would reveal the beat sheet mapped almost one-to-one. The formula is serving the story, but the story has not yet transcended the formula.

**4 (Strong):** The beat sheet's pacing logic is fully absorbed. The novel moves with commercial efficiency -- each section does its job, the "Fun and Games" section delivers the premise's promise, the "All Is Lost" moment creates genuine despair. The beats feel like the story's natural rhythm rather than imposed structure.
> Most successful commercial thrillers and romantic comedies operate at this level -- the Save the Cat structure is invisible, but the pacing it produces is felt. The reader is carried through the story by beats that feel organic because the writer has tailored the template to the material.

**5 (Masterful):** The beat sheet's commercial pacing is achieved with such apparent effortlessness that it feels like discovery rather than architecture. Every beat serves both plot and theme. The "Opening Image / Final Image" bookend creates thematic resonance. The "B-Story" enriches rather than distracts from the "A-Story." The formula has been transcended -- the writer has internalized its wisdom and expressed it in a form unique to this story.
> Snyder himself cites *Miss Congeniality* and *Legally Blonde* as clean realizations of his system. For literary applications, *Gone Girl* by Gillian Flynn uses commercial pacing with literary ambition -- the beats land with precision while the unreliable narration subverts the reader's trust in the very structure that is carrying them.

### Truby's 22 Steps

**1 (Absent):** Character and structure are disconnected. The protagonist's moral growth (Truby's central concern) does not drive the plot. The need, desire, and self-revelation are unclear or absent. Steps are missing or feel imposed. The protagonist changes because the story is over, not because the events have genuinely forced a reckoning.
> A protagonist who has a "self-revelation" in the final chapter that is unconnected to anything they have experienced. The story happened to them; it did not grow from them. Truby in *The Anatomy of Story*: "All good stories are organic. They grow from a single seed, which is the premise, and every part is connected to every other part."

**2 (Developing):** The character's need and desire are present, and some structural events serve the moral argument. But the connection is inconsistent -- some steps feel character-driven, others feel like plot mechanisms. The self-revelation exists but does not feel fully earned by the preceding events.
> A novel where the protagonist clearly wants one thing and needs another, but the plot does not systematically force the confrontation between want and need. The Truby steps are partially realized.

**3 (Competent):** The core Truby logic is present -- need, desire, opponent, and self-revelation are identifiable and connected. The protagonist's moral journey drives the main arc. But not all structural events serve the moral argument equally, and some steps feel more mechanical than organic. The self-revelation is earned but not inevitable -- the reader can see how the events led to it without feeling that no other outcome was possible.
> A novel where the protagonist's need and desire are in clear tension, the opponent forces genuine confrontation, and the self-revelation resolves the moral argument. But two or three plot events along the way serve pacing or suspense rather than the character's moral journey. The framework is mostly realized with a few seams showing.

**4 (Strong):** Character arc and plot structure are unified. The protagonist's need drives the story's moral argument. The self-revelation is earned by the accumulation of events. Each major plot point can be traced back to the character's nature. The 22 steps may not all be present, but the core logic (need, desire, opponent, self-revelation) is fully realized.
> *Breaking Bad* (novelistic television) -- Truby's framework maps cleanly onto Walter White's arc. Every escalation is driven by character need and desire. The self-revelation (which White refuses) is earned by every preceding step.

**5 (Masterful):** The Truby framework is so deeply integrated that plot and character arc are indistinguishable. The protagonist's moral growth (or tragic refusal to grow) IS the plot. The self-revelation (or its refusal) IS the climax. Every structural event serves the character's journey from weakness to self-knowledge (or self-destruction). The story feels like a living organism rather than an assembled structure.
> *Crime and Punishment* -- Raskolnikov's need (to recognize his own humanity), desire (to prove himself exceptional), and self-revelation (at Sonya's feet) form a perfect Truby arc. The structure is entirely character-driven; removing Raskolnikov would not leave a plot behind.

### Harmon's Story Circle

**1 (Absent):** The eight-step cycle is either absent or mechanical. The character's "zone of comfort" is unclear. The "want" is vague. The "unfamiliar situation" is not genuinely unfamiliar. The "heavy price" is not genuinely heavy. The "change" is not genuinely transformative. The circle does not close -- the character at the end does not feel like a transformed version of the character at the beginning.
> A chapter that follows a character through a vaguely uncomfortable experience and returns them to their starting position without change. The circle's structure is present but its emotional logic is not.

**2 (Developing):** The cycle is recognizable and the major beats land, but some transitions are weak. The "want" is clear but the "price" is insufficient. Or the "change" is told rather than demonstrated. The circle closes but the closure feels rushed or unearned.
> A chapter where the character enters an unfamiliar situation with clear desire but then "gets what they want" too easily. The price should be heavy; instead it is a minor inconvenience. The circle's shape is right but its emotional weight is wrong.

**3 (Competent):** The eight steps are present and most carry emotional weight. The character's comfort zone is established, the want is specific, the unfamiliar situation is genuinely challenging. But one or two steps are underpowered -- perhaps the "price" is real but not heavy enough, or the "change" is visible but not deeply felt. The circle closes and the reader senses transformation, but the transformation does not resonate as strongly as it could.
> A chapter-level story circle where the character's journey is clear and the return feels meaningfully different from the departure. But the "search" and "find" steps blur together, or the "heavy price" is a consequence the character accepts too easily. The shape is right and most of the weight is right, but one section of the circle is thin.

**4 (Strong):** The eight steps are realized with emotional truth. The character's comfort zone is vivid. The want is specific and compelling. The unfamiliar situation is genuinely challenging. The price is heavy. The change is real and visible. The circle closes with the reader feeling that the character has been fundamentally altered by the journey -- even if that journey is a single chapter.
> Harmon describes the circle as fractal -- applicable to entire stories and to individual episodes/chapters. A strong chapter-level circle creates the feeling of a complete mini-journey within the larger arc.

**5 (Masterful):** The story circle operates at multiple levels simultaneously -- the chapter is a circle, the act is a circle, the novel is a circle. Each level's "change" feeds into the next level's "comfort zone." The fractal quality of the structure creates a sense of deepening transformation. The character who "returns" to the familiar at the end of the novel is so changed that the familiar itself has been transformed.
> *A Christmas Carol* by Dickens -- Scrooge's journey is a perfect story circle (comfort/want/go/search/find/take/return/change) executed with such precision that it has become the cultural template for transformation narratives. The "change" is so thorough that the return to the familiar (Christmas morning, his own bedroom) becomes the most joyful moment in the story.

### Kishotenketsu

**1 (Absent):** The four-part structure is either absent or misunderstood. There is no genuine "twist" (ten) -- the third movement does not introduce a new element that recontextualizes the first two. Alternatively, the structure is used as an excuse for plotlessness -- the absence of conflict is not replaced by juxtaposition, atmosphere, or thematic resonance but by emptiness. The reconciliation (ketsu) does not harmonize because there is nothing to harmonize.
> A section with four parts that are simply four consecutive things that happen, with no recontextualization in the third part and no harmonization in the fourth. The writer has divided the material into quarters without understanding the structure's logic.

**2 (Developing):** The four parts are identifiable and the "twist" introduces something new, but the recontextualization is weak -- the new element does not genuinely change how the reader understands the first two parts. The reconciliation exists but feels incomplete. The writer is attempting the structure but its unique power is not yet realized.
> A section where Ki and Sho establish a character in a setting, Ten introduces a new character, but the new character does not reframe what came before -- they simply add to it. The structure is present but the recontextualization is missing.

**3 (Competent):** The four-part structure functions and the "twist" creates some degree of recontextualization. The reader's understanding of the first two parts shifts when the third arrives. The reconciliation brings the elements together into something coherent. But the recontextualization is moderate rather than revelatory -- the reader thinks "ah, I see" rather than experiencing a genuine shift in perception. The structure's unique power is partially realized.
> A section where Ki establishes a quiet domestic scene, Sho deepens it, and Ten introduces an element that reframes the domesticity as fragile or threatened. The recontextualization works, but it is the kind of shift the reader half-expected rather than a genuine surprise. Ketsu harmonizes, but the harmony is comfortable rather than profound.

**4 (Strong):** The four-part structure creates meaning through juxtaposition. Ki establishes. Sho deepens. Ten introduces a genuinely new element that forces the reader to reconsider the first two parts. Ketsu harmonizes all elements into a new understanding. The absence of conflict-as-engine is not a weakness but a feature -- the section achieves emotional or thematic resonance through a different mechanism.
> Sections of *Piranesi* by Susanna Clarke operate on kishotenketsu logic -- the beauty of the House is established, deepened, then recontextualized by a revelation that harmonizes into new understanding. The structure creates wonder rather than conflict.

**5 (Masterful):** The kishotenketsu structure achieves effects that conflict-based structures cannot. The "twist" recontextualizes everything that came before with the force of revelation. The reconciliation creates a new understanding that is richer than any resolution through conflict could be. The reader experiences a shift in perception rather than a resolution of tension -- and that shift is more profound than any climax.
> The film *Spirited Away* by Miyazaki (applicable to novelistic structure) -- the four-part structure creates meaning through encounter and transformation rather than through conflict and resolution. In prose, sections of *The Pillow Book* by Sei Shonagon achieve kishotenketsu through juxtaposition of observations that, taken together, produce an understanding none could produce alone.

### Freytag's Pyramid

**1 (Absent):** The energy shape of the act or novel does not follow any discernible pattern. There is no building tension, no identifiable climax, and no meaningful resolution. Alternatively, the pyramid is inverted -- the most intense material is at the beginning, and the act deflates from there. The reader feels no sense of rising stakes or building momentum.
> An act that opens with a dramatic confrontation and then spends sixty pages on lower-stakes material. The energy shape is a cliff rather than a pyramid. The reader's engagement diminishes as the pages increase.

**2 (Developing):** Rising action is present but uneven. The climax is identifiable but underpowered or misplaced. Falling action is either rushed or absent -- the writer treats the climax as the end rather than recognizing that the post-climax space is where meaning crystallizes. The pyramid's shape is visible but its proportions are off.
> An act with good rising action that builds to a climax but then ends abruptly. The falling action -- where the reader processes what happened and meaning settles -- is missing. The reader feels the rush but not the resonance.

**3 (Competent):** The energy shape is recognizable and generally well-proportioned. Rising action builds, the climax arrives at a reasonable moment, and falling action provides some space for processing. But the curve lacks force -- the rise is steady rather than accelerating, the climax is clear rather than explosive, the falling action is present rather than resonant. The pyramid works as architecture without generating maximum emotional force.
> An act with a clear energy arc where the reader can feel the shape of the story. The rising action moves upward, the climax peaks, the resolution settles. But the experience is more "satisfying" than "powerful" -- the shape is correct without being thrilling.

**4 (Strong):** The energy shape is well-proportioned. Rising action builds steadily. The climax arrives with force and at the right moment. Falling action allows the reader to process, and resolution provides closure (or deliberate openness). The reader experiences the satisfying shape of narrative tension building, cresting, and resolving.
> Freytag developed his model from Shakespeare and Greek drama. A strong realization matches what he described: exposition that orients without delaying, rising action that builds without rushing, a climax that peaks without premature arrival, and falling action that resolves without dragging.

**5 (Masterful):** The energy shape is not merely correct but expressive. The particular shape of this act's pyramid -- how steep the rise, how sharp the peak, how long the falling action -- is tailored to this story's needs. A thriller may have a steep, sharp pyramid. A literary novel may have a gradual rise and extended falling action where meaning accumulates. The shape IS the story's emotional argument.
> *King Lear* -- the rising action builds through five acts to a climax of almost unbearable intensity (the storm, the blinding of Gloucester, Cordelia's death), followed by falling action that is not denouement but devastation. The pyramid's shape -- long rise, sharp peak, steep fall -- mirrors Lear's trajectory from power to nothingness. Similarly, *Anna Karenina* uses an asymmetric pyramid where the falling action is extended, allowing Tolstoy to explore the consequences and meaning of the climactic choices.

## Tropes and Genre

These four principles are scored per-novel.

### Trope Awareness

**1 (Absent):** The writer deploys tropes unconsciously. Stock characters, predictable plot turns, and inherited narrative patterns appear without examination. The novel reads as an assembly of familiar parts rather than a considered use of tradition. The writer does not seem to know that their "original" ideas are well-worn conventions.
> A fantasy novel with the Chosen One, the Wise Mentor, the Dark Lord, and the Ragtag Band of Misfits, deployed without any awareness that these are tropes with long histories. The novel reproduces the pattern without engaging with it.

**2 (Developing):** The writer is aware of some tropes and attempts to do something with them, but the engagement is uneven. Some tropes are handled with awareness; others are deployed without examination. The writer's trope consciousness is emerging but not yet consistent.
> A novel that subverts the Chosen One trope (the prophecy is wrong) but plays the Love Triangle completely straight, unaware that it is a trope requiring the same level of intentionality.

**3 (Competent):** The writer demonstrates awareness of most tropes they are using and engages with them intentionally more often than not. Tropes are generally played straight for a reason or tweaked to fit the story's specific needs. But some tropes are still used on autopilot -- the writer's awareness extends to the major patterns but not to every inherited convention in the work. The engagement is genuine but not yet comprehensive.
> A novel that handles its central tropes with clear intentionality -- the mentor is given depth, the quest is complicated by moral ambiguity -- but uses several background tropes (the loyal sidekick, the love interest who waits at home) without the same level of examination. The writer's awareness has a center and a periphery.

**4 (Strong):** The writer demonstrates consistent awareness of the tropes they are using. Each trope is deployed intentionally -- played straight for a reason, subverted for a reason, or avoided for a reason. The reader can tell that the writer knows the tradition they are working in and has made conscious choices about how to engage with it.
> A novel that uses the "enemies to lovers" trope with full awareness, giving both characters genuine reasons for enmity and genuine reasons for attraction, rather than relying on the trope to do the emotional work. The trope is a starting point, not a substitute for characterization.

**5 (Masterful):** Every trope in the novel is deployed with full awareness and serves the story's unique purposes. Familiar patterns feel fresh because the writer has found what is specific and alive within the convention. The reader experiences the pleasure of recognition (the trope) and the pleasure of surprise (what this writer does with it) simultaneously.
> Terry Pratchett's Discworld novels demonstrate masterful trope awareness -- every fantasy convention is simultaneously honored and examined. *The Left Hand of Darkness* by Le Guin takes the "first contact" trope and uses it to examine gender, politics, and human connection in ways that the trope had never been used for before.

### Archetype vs. Cliche

**1 (Absent):** Characters are cliches -- stock figures drained of specificity. The Wise Mentor, the Manic Pixie Dream Girl, the Troubled But Brilliant Detective. These characters exist as types rather than as people. They have no specificity that distinguishes them from every other character who fills the same role. The reader has met these characters many times before and learns nothing new from meeting them again.
> A wise old wizard who dispenses cryptic wisdom, trains the hero, and dies at the dramatically convenient moment. Nothing about this specific wizard distinguishes him from Gandalf, Dumbledore, or Obi-Wan. He is a function, not a person.

**2 (Developing):** Characters begin as archetypes and show some individuality, but the individuality is superficial -- a distinctive quirk or backstory detail bolted onto a stock figure. The character is recognizable as a type with decorations rather than as a person who happens to fill an archetypal role. The writer is trying to differentiate but has not yet found the depth.
> A mentor figure with an unusual hobby (birdwatching) and a tragic backstory (lost a child) but whose function in the story is identical to every other mentor. The details add color but do not transform the cliche into a person.

**3 (Competent):** Characters occupy archetypal roles with enough individuality to feel like distinct people rather than stock types. The reader engages with them as characters rather than recognizing them purely as functions. But the specificity, while real, does not yet reach the level where the archetype disappears entirely -- the reader can still see the role beneath the person. The characters have crossed the line from cliche to competent characterization but have not yet reached the territory where archetype and individual are inseparable.
> A mentor character who has genuine personality, specific motivations, and moments that surprise the reader. The character is not a stock figure, but the reader still thinks of them as "the mentor" rather than as a person who happens to teach the protagonist. The individuality is real but the role is still visible.

**4 (Strong):** Characters occupy archetypal roles but are specific enough to feel like individuals. The reader recognizes the archetype but engages with the character as a person first. The path from cliche to archetype has been traveled through specificity -- particular details, contradictions, and behaviors that make this character irreplaceable.
> Haymitch Abernathy in *The Hunger Games* -- the mentor archetype, but his alcoholism, his bitterness, and his particular way of caring (through strategic cruelty) make him a specific person rather than a function. He fills the role but exceeds it.

**5 (Masterful):** Characters tap into archetypal resonance while being utterly specific. They feel simultaneously universal and unique. The reader recognizes something primal in the character (the trickster, the threshold guardian, the shadow) while also recognizing someone they have never met before. The archetype is alive because it has been made particular.
> Beloved in Morrison's *Beloved* -- she is simultaneously a ghost, a lost child, the embodiment of historical trauma, and a specific, terrifying, needy young woman. She occupies multiple archetypal registers (the revenant, the devouring child, the return of the repressed) while being irreducibly particular. Similarly, Gollum in *The Lord of the Rings* -- the shadow self, the addiction, the pitiable monster -- is archetypal and utterly specific, from his speech patterns to his relationship with fish.

### Genre Contract

**1 (Absent):** The novel violates its genre's core promise without awareness or intention. A romance that does not resolve the central relationship. A mystery that does not solve the puzzle. A horror novel that is not frightening. The reader who chose the genre for its promised experience is betrayed. The violation is not subversion -- it is failure to deliver.
> A novel marketed as a thriller that has no suspense, no escalating danger, and no time pressure. The genre label creates an expectation the novel does not fulfill. The reader does not feel subverted; they feel cheated.

**2 (Developing):** The genre contract is mostly honored but with significant wobbles. A romance that delivers the emotional ending but does not give the relationship enough page time. A mystery that solves the puzzle but introduces the crucial clue too late. The promise is kept in broad strokes but the delivery is uneven.
> A horror novel that builds effective dread for two hundred pages but deflates with an explanation that is more science-fiction than horror. The contract is honored in letter but not in spirit.

**3 (Competent):** The genre contract is fulfilled. The core promise is delivered -- the mystery is solved, the romance resolves, the thriller thrills. Genre readers will find what they came for. But the delivery is workmanlike rather than exceptional -- the conventions are honored without being elevated. The reader finishes the book satisfied but not surprised by what the genre could do.
> A mystery where the puzzle is fair, the clues are planted, and the solution is satisfying. The reader feels the contract was honored. But the novel does not push the genre's boundaries or offer anything the reader could not have found in a dozen other competent genre novels. The promise is kept; it is not exceeded.

**4 (Strong):** The genre contract is fulfilled with confidence. The core promise is delivered. Readers of the genre will find what they came for -- the emotional satisfaction, the intellectual puzzle, the thrilling escalation, the romantic fulfillment. Convention is honored where it serves the reader and stretched where it serves the story.
> A well-executed genre novel that delivers its promise: the mystery is fair and surprising, the romance is earned and satisfying, the horror escalates to a genuinely frightening climax. The reader's trust is rewarded.

**5 (Masterful):** The genre contract is fulfilled while also expanding what the genre can do. The novel delivers the core promise with such skill that genre readers are deeply satisfied, while also offering something they did not expect -- thematic depth, stylistic innovation, emotional complexity that transcends the genre's usual range. The novel honors the contract and transcends it simultaneously.
> *The Remains of the Day* by Ishiguro -- fulfills the literary novel's contract (psychological depth, thematic resonance, beautiful prose) while also functioning as a perfectly constructed romance in which the love story is expressed entirely through what is not said. *Gone Girl* fulfills the thriller contract (suspense, twists, page-turning momentum) while also being a savage examination of marriage and performance. Both novels give genre readers exactly what they want and more than they expected.

### Subversion, Deconstruction, and Reconstruction

**1 (Absent):** The novel attempts subversion or deconstruction but the attempt is shallow. "Subversion" consists of inverting a trope without understanding why the trope exists (the chosen one fails -- that is the whole idea). "Deconstruction" consists of making everything dark and cynical without genuine examination of the trope's assumptions. "Reconstruction" is absent -- the novel tears down without building anything in its place. The reader does not gain new understanding from the subversion.
> A "dark" fairy tale retelling where the princess rescues herself and the prince is useless. This is inversion, not subversion -- it swaps the roles without examining the assumptions that created them.

**2 (Developing):** The subversion or deconstruction has genuine insight but is inconsistently applied. Some tropes are examined with intelligence; others are subverted superficially. The writer's critical lens is present but selectively applied -- the engagement with genre conventions is uneven.
> A novel that deconstructs the "mentor" trope intelligently (examining what it means for an older person to shape a younger person's destiny) but plays the "chosen one" trope straight without the same level of examination. The critical lens is present but selectively applied.

**3 (Competent):** The novel engages with tropes at a level beyond naive use. Subversions are meaningful and deconstructions examine genuine assumptions. The engagement with genre conventions is consistent across most of the work. But the subversion or deconstruction does not yet achieve the depth that would make it revelatory -- the reader thinks "that's a smart take" rather than "I will never see this trope the same way." The critical engagement is real but has not yet transformed the reader's relationship to the material.
> A novel that deconstructs the hero's journey by giving the hero genuine doubts and showing the cost of heroism on the hero's relationships. The deconstruction is intelligent and consistent, but it is a familiar kind of intelligence -- the reader has seen this move before, even if it is executed well. The engagement with the genre is thoughtful without being transformative.

**4 (Strong):** The novel engages with tropes at a sophisticated level. Subversions are meaningful -- they create new understanding rather than simply inverting expectations. Deconstructions examine the assumptions underlying tropes. Reconstructions, if present, acknowledge the critique and rebuild on stronger foundations. The reader's relationship with the genre's conventions is enriched.
> *Watchmen* by Alan Moore (applicable to novelistic analysis) -- deconstructs the superhero at every level while ultimately arguing that heroism, stripped of fantasy, is still meaningful. The deconstruction and reconstruction operate simultaneously.

**5 (Masterful):** The novel moves through the maturation cycle with complete control. Subversion surprises and illuminates. Deconstruction exposes assumptions the reader did not know they held. Reconstruction reassembles the trope in a form that is stronger for having been examined. The reader emerges from the novel with a transformed relationship to the genre's conventions -- able to see both what the conventions offer and what they conceal.
> *Don Quixote* by Cervantes -- simultaneously the first novel and the first deconstruction of the romance genre. It examines why people need heroic narratives, what happens when those narratives collide with reality, and why the collision is both comic and tragic. The reconstruction: Quixote's idealism, though delusional, is more beautiful than the world's cynicism. Similarly, *The Princess Bride* by William Goldman -- simultaneously a fairy tale, a subversion of fairy tales, a deconstruction of storytelling itself, and a reconstruction that argues love stories work precisely because they are impossible.
