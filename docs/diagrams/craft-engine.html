Storyforge Reference

The Craft Engine

The Craft Engine is Storyforge's foundational reference -- a codification of storytelling, prose, and character craft principles that threads through every skill and interaction. It does not prescribe a single method. It treats every framework as a lens: pick the one that reveals what the draft is hiding, then set it down. Craft is teachable. Taste is personal. The engine teaches craft.

03 . Character Craft

Character Craft

Eight frameworks for building characters who feel inevitable on the page.

Want / Need Framework

The gap between what a character wants and what they need is the engine of arc. Want drives scenes. Need drives the story.

Wound and the Lie

Something happened before page one. A false belief born from that wound shapes every decision the character makes.

Character as Theme

Each major character embodies a different answer to the story's central question.

Egri's Premise

The character trait IS the cause of the plot. Character and story are inseparable.

The Character Web

Define every character in relation to every other. Truby's approach: no character exists in isolation.

Flaws as Strengths

Stubbornness is loyalty pushed too far. Recklessness is courage without restraint. Every strength has a shadow.

Voice as Character

What they notice, what metaphors they reach for, what they leave unsaid. Voice reveals character before dialogue does.

Diagnostic Questions

"What does this character do when alone?" "What do they lie about?" Questions that excavate who they truly are.

04 . The Rules

The Rules and When to Break Them

Seven craft rules, each explained with why it exists -- and the specific conditions under which to deliberately violate it.

The Rule
"Show, Don't Tell"

Dramatize through action, sensation, and detail rather than stating emotions or facts directly. Let readers experience the story.

When to Break It

When compressing time across months or years. When the telling voice itself is the stylistic point. When showing would belabor something the reader already understands.

The Rule
"Avoid Adverbs"

Adverbs often signal a weak verb. Strengthen the verb instead of modifying it.

When to Break It

When the adverb contradicts expectation -- "smiled coldly," "whispered fiercely." The contradiction creates meaning the verb alone cannot carry.

The Rule
"Avoid Passive Voice"

Active voice is direct, energetic, and clear about who acts.

When to Break It

When the receiver matters more than the actor. "The village was destroyed" centers destruction, not destroyer -- sometimes that is the point.

The Rule
"Write What You Know"

Ground fiction in authentic experience and observed truth.

When to Break It

Write from emotional truth, not autobiography. You need not have fought a war to write grief. Research plus empathy extends what you "know" beyond lived experience.

The Rule
"Never Open with Weather / Dreams / Waking"

These openings delay character and conflict. They have become cliche through overuse.

When to Break It

When weather IS the conflict. When the dream contains information no other scene can deliver. When waking is the inciting event.

The Rule
"Avoid Said-Bookisms"

"Said" is invisible to readers. Alternatives like "exclaimed" or "retorted" draw attention to themselves.

When to Break It

Sparingly, when the alternative carries specific meaning that context and dialogue alone cannot convey.

The Rule
"Kill Your Darlings"

Be willing to cut any passage, no matter how beloved, if it does not serve the whole.

When to Break It

The rule asks for willingness, not obligation. It demands you be capable of cutting anything -- not that you must cut everything. Keep the darling if it earns its place.

05 . Tropes and Genre

Tropes and Genre Conventions

How to think about the recurring patterns of storytelling -- as tools, not traps.

How to Think About Tropes

Tools, not traps. The question is not whether you use a trope but whether you use it consciously. "Am I using this trope, or is this trope using me?"

Archetype vs. Cliche

Specificity is the path from cliche to archetype. A cliche is a pattern drained of meaning through repetition. An archetype is a pattern renewed through specific, lived detail.

Genre Conventions as Reader Contracts

Every genre carries implicit promises. Romance promises emotional resolution. Mystery promises revelation. Violating the contract requires earning the reader's trust first.

06 . Coaching Posture

The Coaching Posture

How Storyforge deploys everything above. Five postures that define the relationship between engine and author.

Challenge

"This villain's motivation is convenient for plot but not convincing."

Suggest

"What if the reveal happens in Ch12 instead of Ch18?"

Explain

When invoking a principle, explain why it matters here -- never cite without context.

Respect

"Did you choose this, or did it happen by accident?" The author's vision is sovereign.

Push

"You can go further here." Encouragement to reach past the comfortable draft.

Taste vs. Craft

Craft is teachable: sentence structure, pacing, scene construction, character logic. Taste is preference: aesthetic values, genre affinity, stylistic voice. Storyforge teaches craft. It does not impose taste.

The Good Coaching Pattern

  1. Name the specific problem
  2. Locate it precisely in the text
  3. Explain why it matters
  4. Suggest a direction (not a solution)
  5. Never diagnose without evidence
