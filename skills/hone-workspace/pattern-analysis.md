## Pattern 1: Metaphor Restatement

An image or metaphor immediately followed by a clause explaining what it means.

**Total: 27 instances across 22 scenes**

### Top 5 Worst Offenders

| Scene | Count |
|-------|-------|
| kirin | 4 |
| burns | 3 |
| deeper | 3 |
| gathering | 2 |
| dex-back | 2 |

### Examples

**Example 1 — kirin (simile + elaboration):**
> "His voice in this form was the same and completely different. The warmth was still there. The charm was gone. What was left was a creature who had been alone for so long that the aloneness had become structural, load-bearing, the thing his whole self was organized around."

The metaphor "aloneness had become structural, load-bearing" is immediately explained with "the thing his whole self was organized around."

**Example 2 — burns (image + em-dash elaboration):**
> "Dex moved more carefully now, like someone aware of edges. The warmth that used to precede him into a room was still there, but banked. An ember where there'd been a blaze."

The image "banked" is restated as "an ember where there'd been a blaze" — the same idea said twice.

**Example 3 — deeper (metaphor + "the kind of" restatement):**
> "I could feel, even without the Sight, that something fundamental had shifted. The immortal fire had spent itself. What remained was mortal. Steady, warm, diminished."

The metaphor "The immortal fire had spent itself" is immediately restated with three adjectives that explain it: "Steady, warm, diminished." And earlier in the same scene: "The kind that comes after something has burned at full intensity and learned it can't sustain that, and chooses to keep burning anyway at whatever level remains" — a full sentence unpacking what "diminished" already conveyed.

## Pattern 2: Interpretive Tagging

A physical action or gesture followed by narrator commentary explaining its significance.

**Total: 34 instances across 26 scenes**

### Top 5 Worst Offenders

| Scene | Count |
|-------|-------|
| betrayal | 4 |
| crew | 3 |
| community | 3 |
| eve | 2 |
| antlers | 2 |

### Examples

**Example 1 — betrayal (action + significance explained):**
> "Oren hadn't moved from the wall. They stood with their back pressed flat against the brick, their hands at their sides, their fingers spread wide like they were trying to hold onto the surface behind them. Their eyes were glassy and unfocused. After the near-erasure, this was what happened when the world became too much: they froze. The body locked up, the breathing went shallow, and Oren went somewhere deep inside where nothing could reach them."

Physical description of Oren's posture, then two full sentences of narrator explaining what the posture means.

**Example 2 — crew (action + "It was a gesture of..." pattern):**
> "He had that quality. Making any space feel like it had been arranged around him."

After describing Dex sitting on a crate "like a throne," the narrator immediately explains the significance — he had "that quality."

**Example 3 — community (action + "as if/as though" interpretation):**
> "Oren's feet had stopped swinging."

This is actually one of the GOOD moments — pure action, no interpretation. But elsewhere in the same scene: "Mal caught my eye from across the room. He tilted his head toward the service tunnel. A question: *Time to go?*" The italicized interpretation tags the gesture explicitly.

## Pattern 3: Landing-the-Plane Ending

Last 3-5 sentences follow the pattern: [small physical action] + [thematic/metaphorical observation] + [short declarative ≤8 words].

**Total: 14 scenes with this pattern (25% of all scenes)**

### Top 5 Most Formulaic Endings

| Scene | Final Lines |
|-------|------------|
| alone | "Suki's fingers tightened on the mug... 'Okay,' she said." |
| dex-back | "He left the bag on the bench." |
| century | "Dex tilted his head. 'Which ferry?'" — actually avoids this. But **lakefront** does it: "...still walking too slow for Chicago." |
| antlers | "Oren drank their tea." |
| after | "Rami handed me a fork, and I ate." |

### Examples

**Example 1 — after:**
> "No. But it is real."
> Rami handed me a fork, and I ate.

Small physical action (handing a fork) + thematic observation ("it is real") + short declarative ("and I ate").

**Example 2 — antlers:**
> "On the screen, something with no eyes drifted past a vent, trailing filaments of light into the dark. Oren drank their tea."

Metaphorical/thematic observation (eyeless deep-sea creature trailing light into dark — mirrors their conversation about the Hunter) + short declarative ("Oren drank their tea").

**Example 3 — alone:**
> "I need to find them," I said. "All of them. And bring them back."
> Suki's fingers tightened on the mug. She looked at me for a long time.
> "Okay," she said.

Physical action (fingers tightening) + beat of silence + short declarative response (two words).

### Note on this pattern

This is the most debatable pattern. Many of these endings are genuinely effective — the "Oren drank their tea" ending in antlers earns its simplicity after an intense emotional conversation. The pattern becomes a problem when it recurs in 14 of 56 scenes (25%), making the endings feel templated. The strongest endings are the ones that break this mold: **welcome** ends with forward motion into the market, **hemorrhage** ends with the single word "Gray." (not preceded by physical action), and **gathering** ends with sustained description of communal singing.

## Top 5 Scenes Most Needing Re-Draft

Ranked by combined pattern count and density (patterns per word).

| Rank | Scene | Metaphor Restatement | Interpretive Tagging | Landing-the-Plane | Total | Notes |
|------|-------|---------------------|---------------------|-------------------|-------|-------|
| 1 | **kirin** | 4 | 1 | 1 | 6 | Highest metaphor restatement count. The reveal scene over-explains nearly every image. |
| 2 | **betrayal** | 1 | 4 | 1 | 6 | Heavy interpretive tagging. Nearly every character gesture gets narrator commentary. |
| 3 | **burns** | 3 | 1 | 1 | 5 | The "after" scene restates Dex's diminishment three different ways. |
| 4 | **deeper** | 3 | 1 | 0 | 4 | The gathering scene's aftermath over-explains the kirin's transformation meaning. |
| 5 | **crew** | 1 | 3 | 0 | 4 | Introductions scene tags every character's behavior with significance. |

### Why these five

**kirin** is the worst offender because its central revelation — Dex showing his true form — should be the most visceral moment in the book, but it is buried under layers of the narrator explaining what every sensory detail means. The line "I couldn't categorize it, couldn't file it alongside any flavor I'd ever encountered. Too much at once. Like biting into something so concentrated your brain stalls and all you can do is stand there with your mouth open while your senses try to catch up" tells us three times that the experience was overwhelming: "couldn't categorize," "too much at once," and the simile about the brain stalling.

**betrayal** interprets almost every character's body language for the reader. Oren freezes and the narrator explains why. Mal's footsteps fold away and we're told the corridor was "quieter by one person." Suki's controlled exit is described, then tagged as "terrible in its control."

**burns** and **deeper** both struggle with the post-Gathering Dex, restating his diminishment ("banked," "ember," "mortal," "steady, warm, diminished," "a different kind of beautiful") when any one of these would suffice.

**crew** introduces the core cast and tags each person's demeanor with narrator commentary about what it reveals about their character, when the behaviors themselves are clear enough.

## Observations

1. **Metaphor restatement is the most pervasive but least damaging pattern.** Most instances involve one extra clause, not full paragraphs. The fix is usually deletion of a single sentence.

2. **Interpretive tagging is the most damaging pattern.** It undermines the first-person voice. Zara is a line cook with sharp observational instincts — she should notice behavior, not psychoanalyze it for the reader. The best moments in the manuscript are when she registers a detail and moves on (Suki's "two words past what needed saying, which for Suki was practically a speech" in burns — efficient, characterful, done).

3. **Landing-the-plane endings are the most formulaic but also the most defensible.** Some of these are genuinely earned (antlers, after, dex-back). The problem is frequency. When 25% of scenes end with the same rhythm, the reader starts anticipating the beat. Varying the final gesture — ending mid-action, ending on dialogue, ending on sensory detail without the physical-action anchor — would break the template.

4. **The cleanest scenes are: alone, before, kitchen, jinn, rami-door, sleepless, singular.** These scenes trust the reader, let actions speak, and resist the urge to interpret. They should be the model for revision.
