## Patterns Eliminated: Before vs After Examples

### 1. Metaphor restatement removed (kirin)

**Before (lines 113-116):** The warmth was built into a five-clause chain, each restating the prior:
> A banked furnace. A thing that had been burning for centuries and was still burning, still giving off heat, but diminished. Dimmer than it should have been. I could feel that too, the gap between what this fire once was and what it was now. Like standing in a cathedral and knowing from the architecture that the ceiling used to be higher.

**After:** The kirin's warmth is described through direct sensory contact without explanatory restatement. The "banked furnace" metaphor chain is gone entirely. Instead:
> His muzzle was velvet and warm and real under my fingers. The warmth went through my hand and up my arm and settled somewhere behind my sternum, and it was the most honest thing I'd ever felt.

The after version trusts a single concrete image (warmth traveling through the body) where the before version stacked five metaphors to over-explain the same idea.

### 2. Interpretive tagging removed (betrayal)

**Before (line 41):**
> Behind me, Oren made a sound. Small, involuntary, the kind of noise someone makes when they take a blow to the stomach.

**After (lines 77-79):** Oren's reaction is handled through dialogue instead of narrator interpretation:
> "Who was first?" Oren's voice from behind me, barely above a whisper. "When you started. Who was the first one you gave it?"

The before version tags the physical response with "the kind of noise someone makes when..." -- the narrator explains what the sound means. The after version lets Oren's question carry the emotional weight directly.

### 3. Landing-the-plane eliminated (burns)

**Before (lines 150-153):**
> Dex picked up a chunk of fallen concrete and turned it in his fingers, studying the iridescent film that coated one side. His reflection warped in the residue, his human face rippling into something fiercer, antlered, before the surface went dark.
> He set the chunk down and wiped his hand on his jeans.

Classic three-beat ending: [small physical action] + [thematic image about identity] + [deflating declarative]. The residue-reflection serves as a mirror for the scene's themes, and the jeans-wiping brings it back to mundane.

**After (lines 129-131):**
> "We're going to need a better plan."
> Oren's hand tightened on his shoulder, and we kept walking.

The after version ends on dialogue and a single action beat. No thematic observation layered on top. The scene stops instead of landing.

## Patterns That Persist in the After Versions

### 1. Interpretive tagging persists (betrayal, after lines 13-14)

> Mal leaned against the tunnel wall to my left, his splinted arm cradled in its sling. He'd carried two of the maps one-handed. He was watching Naji with the particular focus of someone calculating distances to exits that didn't exist in this room.

"The particular focus of someone calculating distances to exits" is interpretive tagging: physical observation + narrator explaining what the looking means. The first-person POV gives this more license than third-person would, but it still follows the [action] + [the kind/particular X of someone who...] template.

### 2. Metaphor restatement persists (kirin, after lines 111-112)

> This was something fierce and ancient and so utterly alone that the aloneness came off him like weather, like the cold wind off the lake, something you couldn't argue with or fix, only feel.

Two similes stacked ("like weather" + "like the cold wind") followed by a clause that restates the point ("something you couldn't argue with or fix"). The before version had the same aloneness-as-structural metaphor but ran it longer; the after version is shorter but still follows the explain-the-image pattern.

### 3. Interpretive tagging persists (burns, after lines 23-24)

> "What kind of residue?" Oren asked from behind me. Their voice had that careful quality, the one that meant they were calculating whether to be scared.

"The one that meant they were calculating whether to be scared" is a textbook interpretive tag: the narrator explains the significance of a vocal quality using "that meant."

## Assessment

The prohibitions helped substantially but unevenly.

**Metaphor restatement saw the biggest improvement (50% reduction).** The before versions, especially the kirin scene, relied heavily on chains of metaphors where each clause restated the previous one in slightly different terms. The after versions tend to pick one image and let it stand. The kirin before had 11 instances; the kirin after has 5. The worst offender -- the five-clause furnace/cathedral chain -- was eliminated entirely.

**Landing-the-plane improved the most proportionally (67% reduction).** Two of three scenes dropped the pattern. The one that retained it (kirin after) uses it more sparingly -- the "fire burned / lake moved / hand was warm" ending is still the classic three-beat structure, but the beats are shorter and less thematically loaded than the before version.

**Interpretive tagging improved the least (28% reduction).** This pattern proved most resistant, particularly in the betrayal scene (7 before, 6 after). The first-person narrator voice creates a natural pull toward interpreting physical actions -- when Zara observes someone's body language, the prose slides easily into explaining what it means. The after versions trimmed some of the most explicit tags ("the kind of noise someone makes when...") but kept subtler ones ("the particular focus of someone calculating..."). This pattern may be partially inherent to the first-person investigative POV rather than purely an AI artifact.

**Overall:** 41% reduction across all patterns. The prohibitions work best against the most mechanical patterns (metaphor chains, three-beat endings) and less well against patterns that overlap with legitimate narrative voice (interpretive tagging in first person). The after prose reads noticeably less over-explained, but the instinct to annotate images and actions with meaning persists at a reduced level.
